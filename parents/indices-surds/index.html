INDICES & SURDS

Powers, roots, and why your child writes \(\sqrt{2}\) instead of 1.414...

1. What Your Child Is Learning

Indices are powers. When you see \(x^3\), it means \(x \times x \times x\). The small raised number is the index (plural: indices). It tells you how many times to multiply the base by itself.

Surds are roots that can't be written as exact decimals. Instead of writing 1.41421356... your child writes \(\sqrt{2}\). Leaving the answer as a surd keeps it exact — no rounding, no loss of precision. This matters in GCSE maths because exam questions often say "give your answer in exact form".

2. How Schools Teach It Now

Students learn six index laws that cover every situation:

  • Multiply: add the powers — \(x^a \times x^b = x^{a+b}\)
  • Divide: subtract the powers — \(x^a \div x^b = x^{a-b}\)
  • Power of a power: multiply the powers — \((x^a)^b = x^{ab}\)
  • Negative index: take the reciprocal — \(x^{-n} = \dfrac{1}{x^n}\)
  • Fractional index: means a root — \(x^{\frac{1}{n}} = \sqrt[n]{x}\)
  • Zero index: anything to the power 0 is 1 — \(x^0 = 1\)

For surds, the key skill is simplifying by finding square factors:

Method

To simplify a surd, find the largest square number that divides into it, then split:

$$\sqrt{50} = \sqrt{25 \times 2} = \sqrt{25} \times \sqrt{2} = 5\sqrt{2}$$

3. Common Mistakes to Watch For

  • Multiplying powers instead of adding: \(x^3 \times x^4 = x^{12}\) is wrong. The correct answer is \(x^{3+4} = x^7\). When you multiply terms, you add the indices.
  • Thinking surds can't be simplified: students often leave \(\sqrt{8}\) as is, but \(8 = 4 \times 2\), so \(\sqrt{8} = 2\sqrt{2}\).
  • Confusing negative indices with negative numbers: \(2^{-3}\) does not equal \(-8\). It equals \(\dfrac{1}{2^3} = \dfrac{1}{8}\). The negative index means "reciprocal", not "make it negative".

4. Try It Together

Grab a piece of paper and try this with your child:

Worked Example — Simplify \(\sqrt{50}\)

Step 1: Find the largest square number that goes into 50. That's 25.

Step 2: Split it: \(50 = 25 \times 2\)

Step 3: Take the root of the square part: \(\sqrt{25} = 5\)

Answer: \(\sqrt{50} = 5\sqrt{2}\)

Quick Index Laws Check

Ask your child: what is \(3^2 \times 3^4\)?

Add the powers: \(3^{2+4} = 3^6 = 729\)
