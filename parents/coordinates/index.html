KS3 Parent Guide

COORDINATES & QUADRANTS

Plotting points on a grid — including when the numbers go negative. Here's the full picture.

WHAT YOUR CHILD IS LEARNING

Your child is learning to describe the position of a point on a grid using two numbers, written as a pair in brackets: \((x, y)\). The first number tells you the horizontal position (left or right), and the second tells you the vertical position (up or down).

In primary school, your child probably worked with coordinates that were all positive — a grid that only covered the top-right area, with both numbers being 1, 2, 3, and so on. At KS3, the grid expands. The axes now cross in the middle, creating four sections, and the numbers can be negative.

This is a big step. It means a point can be to the left of the centre (negative x) or below the centre (negative y), or both. The grid now has four regions, called quadrants.

The two lines that form the grid are called axes. The horizontal one is the x-axis; the vertical one is the y-axis. The point where they cross — right in the middle — is called the origin, and its coordinates are \((0, 0)\).

HOW SCHOOLS TEACH IT NOW

The Golden Rule: Along the Corridor, Up the Stairs

This is the memory trick that every maths teacher uses, and it works brilliantly. To plot a point like \((3, 5)\):

  • First, go along the corridor (the x-axis) — that's the first number. Positive means right, negative means left.
  • Then go up the stairs (the y-axis) — that's the second number. Positive means up, negative means down.

The order matters. \((3, 5)\) and \((5, 3)\) are completely different points. The first number is always horizontal, the second is always vertical.

The Four Quadrants

The four sections of the grid are numbered 1 to 4, going anticlockwise from the top-right:

QuadrantSignsPosition
1(+, +)Top-right
2(-, +)Top-left
3(-, -)Bottom-left
4(+, -)Bottom-right

Your child doesn't need to memorise this table. Once they understand the signs (negative x = left, negative y = down), the quadrants follow naturally. But knowing they're numbered anticlockwise from the top-right can help in exam questions that reference quadrants by number.

Reading Coordinates from a Grid

Students also practise the reverse: looking at a point on a grid and writing down its coordinates. The process is the same in reverse — read across to the x-axis first, then read up or down to find the y value.

Plotting Shapes

A common extension is to plot several points and join them to make a shape. "Plot (2, 1), (5, 1), (5, 4), and (2, 4). What shape have you made?" (A rectangle.) This builds towards later work on transformations and straight-line graphs.

COMMON MISTAKES TO WATCH FOR

  • Swapping x and y. This is the most common error by far. Students go up first and across second, getting the point in the wrong place. "Along the corridor, up the stairs" puts x first every time. If your child writes \((3, 5)\) but plots it at \((5, 3)\), this is the issue.
  • Forgetting negative signs. When working in all four quadrants, students sometimes drop the minus sign, plotting \((-3, 4)\) as \((3, 4)\). This puts the point in completely the wrong quadrant (quadrant 1 instead of quadrant 2). Double-checking the signs is worth the extra second.
  • Plotting in the wrong quadrant. Even when students get the right numbers, they sometimes count in the wrong direction. A negative x means left of the origin, and a negative y means below the origin. If both are negative, the point is in the bottom-left (quadrant 3).
  • Confusing the origin. Some students think \((0, 0)\) means "nowhere" or leave it off. The origin is a real point — it's where the two axes cross, right in the centre of the grid.

TRY IT TOGETHER

Worked Example

Plot the point \((-3, 4)\) on a coordinate grid.

Step 1: Start at the origin \((0, 0)\).

Step 2: The x-coordinate is \(-3\). Go 3 left along the x-axis (negative means left).

Along the corridor: 3 steps left

Step 3: The y-coordinate is \(4\). Go 4 up (positive means up).

Up the stairs: 4 steps up

Mark the point. It sits in quadrant 2 (top-left), because x is negative and y is positive.

Try these together on a piece of squared paper. Draw the two axes crossing in the middle, number them from -6 to 6 in both directions, then take turns calling out coordinates for the other person to plot. Mix in some negative numbers. It's surprisingly satisfying when you both get the same answer.

Bonus: Which Quadrant?

Without plotting, can you work out which quadrant each point falls in?

\((4, 2)\) → both positive → Quadrant 1 (top-right)
\((-1, -5)\) → both negative → Quadrant 3 (bottom-left)
\((6, -3)\) → positive x, negative y → Quadrant 4 (bottom-right)

Once your child can do this without a grid, they've really understood how the sign of each coordinate maps to position.

A good challenge to finish: "I'm thinking of a point in quadrant 2. What do you know about it?" (Answer: the x-coordinate must be negative and the y-coordinate must be positive.) This kind of reasoning question is exactly what schools are building towards.
