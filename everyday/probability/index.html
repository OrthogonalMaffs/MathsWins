MODULE 1: BASE RATE NEGLECT

The most common probability error in medicine, law, and everyday life.

THE MEDICAL TEST PROBLEM

A disease affects 1 in 1,000 people. A test is 99% accurate. You test positive. What's the probability you actually have the disease?

Most people say 99%. The answer is approximately 9%.

This isn't a trick. It's a genuine failure of human intuition. Almost everyone — including many doctors — gets this wrong.

THE CALCULATION

Imagine 100,000 people tested:

100,000 people
Have disease: 100 | Don't: 99,900

Of the 100 with disease: 99 test positive (true +), 1 test negative
Of the 99,900 without: 98,901 test negative, 999 test positive (false +)

Total positive: 99 + 999 = 1,098
Actually ill: 99

P(ill | positive) = 99 / 1,098 = 9.0%
Out of 1,098 positive tests, only 99 are genuine. The other 999 are false alarms. The base rate (1 in 1,000) is so low that false positives vastly outnumber true positives. This is why screening programmes retest positive results.
The lesson: Test accuracy is meaningless without the base rate. A 99% accurate test for a rare condition produces mostly false positives. A 99% accurate test for a common condition produces mostly true positives.

MODULE 2: BAYES' THEOREM

The formula for updating beliefs when you get new evidence.

THE FORMULA

P(A|B) = P(B|A) × P(A) / P(B)

The probability of A given B =
(probability of B given A) × (prior probability of A)
÷ (total probability of B)
Bayes' theorem is the mathematical framework for updating beliefs with new information. The medical test in Module 1 is a direct application: P(A) is the base rate, P(B|A) is the test accuracy, P(B) is the total probability of a positive result, and P(A|B) is what you want to know.

APPLIED TO THE MEDICAL TEST

P(ill | pos) = P(pos | ill) × P(ill) / P(pos)
= 0.99 × 0.001 / (0.99×0.001 + 0.01×0.999)
= 0.00099 / 0.01098
= 9.02%

BEYOND MEDICINE

The same logic applies everywhere: criminal justice, spam filters, weather prediction. The common error is confusing P(B|A) with P(A|B).

Example: P(ground wet | raining) ≈ 100%. But P(raining | ground wet) is much lower — sprinklers, car washing, burst pipes all produce wet ground. Bayes' theorem quantifies exactly how much evidence should shift your belief.

MODULE 3: THE MONTY HALL PROBLEM

Three doors. One car. Two goats. Should you switch?

THE SETUP

You pick a door. The host opens another door to reveal a goat. He asks: switch to the remaining door?

Always switch. Switching gives 2/3 chance. Staying gives 1/3.

This feels wrong. Two doors, car behind one — surely 50/50? But the host's action gives you information that changes the probabilities.

WHY SWITCHING WINS

Original pick: 1/3 chance of car.
Other two doors: 2/3 chance combined.

Host opens one (always a goat).
The 2/3 concentrates on the remaining door.

Stay: win if original was right → 1/3
Switch: win if original was wrong → 2/3
When you first picked, you were probably wrong (2/3 chance). The host eliminated one wrong option. If you were originally wrong (which you probably were), the car must be behind the remaining door. Switching wins 2 out of 3 times.

MODULE 4: THE BIRTHDAY PROBLEM

How many people for a 50% chance two share a birthday? Just 23.

THE MATHS

P(no match among n people) =
365/365 × 364/365 × ... × (365−n+1)/365

n=23: P(match) = 50.7%
n=30: 70.6% | n=50: 97.0% | n=70: 99.9%
People expect ~183 (half of 365). The answer is 23. You're not looking for someone with YOUR birthday — you're looking for ANY pair. With 23 people there are 253 possible pairs. That's 253 chances for a match.
The principle: When there are many opportunities for a match, probability rises much faster than intuition suggests. This applies to hash collisions in computing, coincidences in life, and why "unlikely" events happen more often than you'd expect.
