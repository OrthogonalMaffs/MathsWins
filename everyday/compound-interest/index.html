MODULE 1: HOW COMPOUNDING WORKS

Interest on your interest. The exponential that most people think linearly about.

SIMPLE VS COMPOUND

Simple interest is calculated only on the original amount. £1,000 at 5% simple interest earns £50 every year, forever. After 10 years you have £1,500.

Compound interest is calculated on the original amount plus all the interest already earned. The interest earns interest. After 10 years at 5% compounded annually, you have £1,628.89 — not £1,500.

Simple interest: A = P × (1 + r × t)
£1,000 × (1 + 0.05 × 10) = £1,500.00

Compound interest: A = P × (1 + r)^t
£1,000 × (1.05)^10 = £1,628.89

Difference: £128.89 — the interest earned on interest
That £128.89 difference is free money — it came from your interest earning its own interest. Over 10 years on £1,000 it's modest. Over 30 years on £100,000 it's transformative. Compounding rewards patience.

TIME IS THE MULTIPLIER

The power of compound interest isn't the rate — it's the time. Watch what happens to £1,000 at 5% over different periods:

After 10 years: £1,000 × 1.05^10 = £1,629
After 20 years: £1,000 × 1.05^20 = £2,653
After 30 years: £1,000 × 1.05^30 = £4,322
After 40 years: £1,000 × 1.05^40 = £7,040
After 50 years: £1,000 × 1.05^50 = £11,467
Your money didn't grow by £200 per decade. It grew by £1,024 in decade one, £1,024 more in decade two, £1,669 in decade three, £2,718 in decade four, and £4,427 in decade five. The growth accelerates because each year there's more money earning interest. This is what exponential growth means — and it's why starting early matters so much.
The key insight: The last 10 years produced more growth than the first 30 combined. Compounding is a patience machine — the reward for waiting is disproportionately large.

MODULE 2: THE CREDIT CARD TRAP

The same exponential that builds your savings destroys your debt — and minimum payments are designed to keep you in it.

HOW MINIMUM PAYMENTS WORK

Most UK credit cards calculate the minimum payment as the greater of:

• A percentage of the balance (typically 2–3%), OR
• A fixed floor (typically £5–£25)

Here's the trap: as your balance drops, your minimum payment drops too. You pay less each month, which means more of each payment goes to interest and less to actually reducing the debt. The balance shrinks slower and slower.

The design is deliberate. Minimum payments are set low enough to keep you paying for as long as possible. A £3,000 balance at 21.9% APR with minimum payments of 2.5% (or £5 floor) takes approximately 28 years to clear and costs roughly £4,800 in interest — making the total cost over £7,800 for something you originally borrowed £3,000 for.

WORKED EXAMPLE: £3,000 AT 21.9% APR

Minimum payment = max(2.5% of balance, £5). Let's trace the first few months and see how the interest eats into your payments.

Month 1:
Balance: £3,000.00
Interest: £3,000 × (21.9% ÷ 12) = £54.75
Minimum payment: £3,000 × 2.5% = £75.00
Principal paid: £75.00 − £54.75 = £20.25
New balance: £2,979.75

Month 2:
Interest: £2,979.75 × 1.825% = £54.38
Minimum: £2,979.75 × 2.5% = £74.49
Principal paid: £20.11
New balance: £2,959.64
In the first month, you paid £75 but only £20.25 actually reduced your debt. The other £54.75 — nearly 73% of your payment — went straight to interest. And the minimum payment drops next month, so even less goes to the balance. This is why it takes 28 years.

FIXED PAYMENTS CHANGE EVERYTHING

If instead of paying the minimum (which shrinks), you fix your payment at £100/month:

£3,000 at 21.9% APR:

Minimum payments only: 28 years, £4,800 interest, £7,800 total
Fixed £100/month: 3 years 2 months, £815 interest, £3,815 total
Fixed £150/month: 2 years, £510 interest, £3,510 total

Saving (£100 vs min): 25 years and £3,985
Paying £100 instead of the minimum saves you £3,985 and 25 years. That's the difference between being debt-free at 30 or still paying at 55. The maths is not subtle.
The rule: Never pay the minimum if you can afford more. Even £20 extra per month makes a dramatic difference. Fix your payment at the highest amount you can sustain, and don't let it shrink as the balance drops.

MODULE 3: APR DECODED

What the Annual Percentage Rate actually means — and why it's not as simple as it sounds.

APR VS INTEREST RATE

APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is designed to be a standardised way to compare borrowing costs. It includes the interest rate plus any mandatory fees, expressed as a yearly figure.

But here's what catches people: credit card interest compounds monthly, not annually. A 21.9% APR doesn't mean you pay 21.9% per year on your balance — it means you pay approximately 1.825% per month, and each month's interest is added to the balance before next month's interest is calculated.

APR: 21.9%
Monthly rate: 21.9% ÷ 12 = 1.825%

Effective annual rate (with monthly compounding):
(1 + 0.219/12)^12 − 1 = (1.01825)^12 − 1 = 24.2%

The actual annual cost is 24.2%, not 21.9%.
A credit card advertising 21.9% APR actually costs you 24.2% per year because of monthly compounding. The APR understates the true cost. This is legal and standard — but it's worth understanding that the number on the tin isn't quite the number in your pocket.

COMPARING LOAN COSTS

APR is most useful for comparing like with like. A personal loan at 6.9% APR is cheaper than a credit card at 21.9% APR — that's clear. But APR doesn't capture everything:

What APR includes: Interest rate, mandatory fees (arrangement fees on loans, for example).
What APR doesn't include: Payment protection insurance (if optional), late payment fees, the effect of paying only the minimum, or the cost of flexibility (credit cards let you borrow and repay flexibly; loans have fixed schedules).

The most honest comparison is total cost of borrowing — the total amount you pay back minus the amount you borrowed. That number tells you exactly what the debt cost you in real money.

£3,000 borrowed for 3 years:

Credit card at 21.9% (min payments): total repaid ≈ £7,800 → cost: £4,800
Credit card at 21.9% (£100/month): total repaid ≈ £3,815 → cost: £815
Personal loan at 6.9% APR (fixed): total repaid ≈ £3,327 → cost: £327
0% balance transfer (24 months): total repaid ≈ £3,090 → cost: £90 fee
Same £3,000, four different approaches, costs ranging from £90 to £4,800. The interest rate matters, but how you repay matters more. A 0% balance transfer with a small fee and disciplined fixed payments is the cheapest option by far.

MODULE 4: THE RULE OF 72

A mental shortcut that estimates how long it takes money to double. Or debt to double.

THE FORMULA

The Rule of 72 is beautifully simple:

Years to double ≈ 72 ÷ interest rate

At 4%: 72 ÷ 4 = 18 years to double
At 6%: 72 ÷ 6 = 12 years to double
At 8%: 72 ÷ 8 = 9 years to double
At 10%: 72 ÷ 10 = 7.2 years to double
At 24%: 72 ÷ 24 = 3 years to double
At a 4% savings rate, your money doubles in about 18 years. At a 24% credit card rate, your debt doubles in about 3 years if you make no payments. Same maths, opposite direction.

WHY IT WORKS

The exact formula for doubling time is:

t = ln(2) ÷ ln(1 + r)

where ln is the natural logarithm and r is the rate as a decimal.

At 6%: t = 0.6931 ÷ ln(1.06) = 0.6931 ÷ 0.0583 = 11.90 years
Rule of 72 estimate: 72 ÷ 6 = 12 years

Close enough for mental arithmetic.
The Rule of 72 is an approximation that works because ln(2) ≈ 0.6931 and for small rates, ln(1 + r) ≈ r. The 72 comes from rounding 69.3 to a more divisible number. It's most accurate between 4% and 12% — which conveniently covers most savings and investment rates.

PRACTICAL APPLICATIONS

For savings: "If my pension grows at 7% per year, it doubles every 10 years. Starting with £50,000 at age 35, that's £100,000 at 45, £200,000 at 55, £400,000 at 65." Three doublings from one mental calculation.
For debt: "My credit card charges 22% APR. 72 ÷ 22 ≈ 3.3 years. If I stop paying entirely, my £2,000 debt becomes £4,000 in just over 3 years." This is why ignoring debt is catastrophically expensive.
For inflation: "Inflation is 3.5%. 72 ÷ 3.5 ≈ 20.6 years. Prices roughly double every 20 years. Something that costs £100 today will cost £200 by 2046." This is why cash savings lose purchasing power over time.
