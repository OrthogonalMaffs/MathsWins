MODULE 1: HOW AMORTISATION WORKS

Why most of your early payments barely touch the debt.

THE PAYMENT FORMULA

Every repayment mortgage uses the same formula to calculate your monthly payment. It looks intimidating but the principle is simple: you pay the same amount every month, and the split between interest and principal shifts over time.

Monthly payment M = P × [r(1+r)ⁿ] / [(1+r)ⁿ − 1]

Where:
P = loan amount (principal)
r = monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12)
n = total number of monthly payments (years × 12)
You don't need to memorise this formula — that's what calculators are for. What matters is understanding what it produces: a fixed monthly payment that starts almost entirely as interest and gradually shifts to almost entirely principal.

WORKED EXAMPLE: £200,000 AT 4.5% OVER 25 YEARS

This is close to a typical UK mortgage. Let's see what the first payment looks like.

Loan: £200,000
Rate: 4.5% annual → 0.375% monthly
Term: 25 years (300 months)

Monthly payment: £1,111.05

Month 1:
Interest: £200,000 × 0.375% = £750.00
Principal: £1,111.05 − £750.00 = £361.05
Remaining balance: £199,638.95

Interest share: 67.5% of your payment
Principal share: 32.5% of your payment
You paid £1,111. Only £361 actually reduced your debt. The other £750 — two thirds of your payment — went straight to the lender as the cost of borrowing. And next month's interest is calculated on £199,639, not £200,000, so the shift is glacially slow at first.

HOW THE SPLIT CHANGES OVER TIME

The payment stays the same every month. But the split between interest and principal shifts dramatically:

Year 1: Interest £8,920 | Principal £4,413 | Balance £195,587
Year 5: Interest £8,394 | Principal £4,939 | Balance £177,016
Year 10: Interest £7,487 | Principal £5,846 | Balance £149,832
Year 15: Interest £6,170 | Principal £7,163 | Balance £116,252
Year 20: Interest £4,289 | Principal £9,044 | Balance £74,133
Year 25: Interest £1,560 | Principal £11,773 | Balance £0

Total paid: £333,315
Total interest: £133,315 (66.7% of the loan amount)
You borrowed £200,000. You paid back £333,315. The extra £133,315 is the price of borrowing — and most of it was paid in the first half of the mortgage. By year 20, your payments are mostly principal. By year 25, interest is a sliver. This is why overpaying early is so powerful — every pound of extra principal in year 1 saves interest for the next 24 years.
The uncomfortable truth: For the first 13 years of a 25-year mortgage at 4.5%, more than half of every payment goes to interest. You don't reach the crossover point — where principal exceeds interest — until you're past the halfway mark of the term.

MODULE 2: THE REAL COST OF TERM LENGTH

Lower monthly payments. Much higher total cost. The trade-off nobody explains.

25 YEARS VS 30 YEARS VS 35 YEARS

Extending your mortgage term reduces monthly payments — that's why people do it. But the total cost difference is enormous.

£200,000 at 4.5%:

25 years: £1,111/month | Total interest: £133,315
30 years: £1,013/month | Total interest: £164,813
35 years: £949/month | Total interest: £198,577

25→30: save £98/month, pay £31,498 more total
25→35: save £162/month, pay £65,262 more total
Extending from 25 to 30 years saves you £98 per month but costs an extra £31,498 in total interest. That's paying £31,498 for the privilege of having £98/month in your pocket. Each extra year of mortgage term costs you roughly £6,300 in additional interest at these rates.
The rule of thumb: Pick the shortest term you can comfortably afford. If cash flow is tight, take the longer term — but overpay when you can. A 30-year term with regular overpayments can end up cheaper than a 25-year term paid at the minimum, because you have flexibility.

THE 40-YEAR MORTGAGE

Increasingly common for first-time buyers in the UK, especially with high property prices. The maths is sobering.

£200,000 at 4.5% over 40 years:

Monthly payment: £906
Total interest: £234,784
Total repaid: £434,784

Compared to 25 years:
Save £205/month
Pay £101,469 more in total interest
A 40-year mortgage on £200,000 at 4.5% costs you £234,784 in interest — more than the amount you borrowed. You pay for the house, and then you pay for it again in interest. The monthly saving of £205 versus a 25-year term costs you over £100,000 in extra interest.

MODULE 3: THE POWER OF OVERPAYMENTS

Small extra payments create disproportionately large savings. Here's why.

WHY OVERPAYMENTS WORK SO WELL

When you overpay, every extra pound goes directly to reducing the principal. This means next month's interest is calculated on a smaller balance. That saved interest itself reduces the balance further the following month. It compounds — the same exponential effect that makes debt expensive makes overpayments powerful.

The earlier you overpay, the more you save. £100 extra in year 1 saves more interest than £100 extra in year 20, because the year-1 overpayment prevents interest being charged on that £100 for the remaining 24 years.

WORKED EXAMPLE: £200/MONTH OVERPAYMENT

£200,000 at 4.5% over 25 years:

Without overpayment:
Monthly: £1,111 | Term: 25 years | Interest: £133,315

With £200/month overpayment:
Monthly: £1,311 | Term: 20 years 7 months | Interest: £110,040

Interest saved: £23,275
Term reduced by: 4 years 5 months
Total extra paid: £200 × 247 months = £49,400
Net benefit: £23,275 saved on £49,400 invested = 47% return
You put in £49,400 extra over the life of the mortgage. You saved £23,275 in interest and got 4.5 years of your life back. That's effectively a 47% return on your overpayments — guaranteed, tax-free, and risk-free. No investment can promise that.
The 10% rule: Most UK fixed-rate mortgages allow overpayments of up to 10% of the outstanding balance per year without penalty. On a £200,000 mortgage, that's up to £20,000 in year one — far more than most people can afford. Check your terms, but you're almost certainly within the limit.

EVEN SMALL AMOUNTS MATTER

£200,000 at 4.5% over 25 years — overpayment comparison:

+£50/month: saves £6,800, clears 1yr 4mo early
+£100/month: saves £12,600, clears 2yr 7mo early
+£200/month: saves £23,275, clears 4yr 5mo early
+£500/month: saves £44,800, clears 8yr 10mo early
Even £50 extra per month — roughly the price of two takeaway coffees per week — saves nearly £7,000 in interest and clears your mortgage over a year early. The returns are non-linear: doubling from £100 to £200 doesn't double the saving, it nearly doubles it. Compounding favours consistency.

MODULE 4: FIXED VS VARIABLE — THE MATHS

Certainty has a price. The question is whether you're willing to pay it.

HOW FIXED RATES WORK

A fixed-rate mortgage locks your interest rate for a set period (typically 2 or 5 years in the UK). Your monthly payment is guaranteed not to change during that period, regardless of what happens to the Bank of England base rate.

The lender is taking a risk by offering you certainty — if rates rise, they lose; if rates fall, you lose. They price this risk into the fixed rate, which is why fixed rates are almost always higher than the current variable rate at the time you take them out.

The premium for certainty: A fixed rate is an insurance policy. You're paying slightly more per month in exchange for knowing exactly what your payment will be. Whether this is worth it depends on your tolerance for uncertainty and your belief about where rates are heading.

HOW VARIABLE AND TRACKER RATES WORK

Standard Variable Rate (SVR): Set by the lender. Can change at any time, for any reason. Usually the most expensive option — this is what you fall onto when your fixed deal expires if you don't remortgage.

Tracker rate: Follows the Bank of England base rate plus a fixed margin. If the base rate is 4.5% and your tracker is base + 0.75%, you pay 5.25%. When the base rate moves, your payment moves by exactly the same amount.

Example: £200,000 mortgage, 25 years

Fixed at 4.5%: £1,111/month (guaranteed 5 years)
Tracker at 4.0%+0.75% = 4.75%: £1,137/month (today)

If base rate drops 0.5%:
Fixed: still £1,111
Tracker: drops to 4.25% → £1,085

If base rate rises 1%:
Fixed: still £1,111
Tracker: rises to 5.75% → £1,246
The fixed rate doesn't move. The tracker moves with every base rate change. If rates fall, the tracker wins. If rates rise, the fixed rate wins. Nobody — not your lender, not the Bank of England, not any economist — knows which way rates will go. The decision is about your personal risk tolerance, not a prediction.

THE REMORTGAGE IMPERATIVE

Never sit on the SVR. When your fixed or tracker deal expires, you're typically moved to the lender's Standard Variable Rate — which can be 1–2% higher than the best available deals. On a £200,000 mortgage, that's £100–£200+ extra per month for no benefit. Remortgaging to a new deal when your current one expires is the single most impactful financial decision most homeowners can make.
£200,000, 20 years remaining:

New fixed at 4.5%: £1,265/month
SVR at 6.5%: £1,491/month

Difference: £226/month = £2,712/year wasted
