MODULE 1: WHAT INFLATION DOES TO YOUR MONEY

£100 today is not £100 in ten years. Here's the maths of purchasing power.

THE PURCHASING POWER FORMULA

Inflation means prices rise over time. If prices rise 3% per year, something that costs £100 today will cost £103 next year. Your £100 buys less. This erosion compounds, just like interest.

Future price = Today's price × (1 + inflation)^years

At 3% inflation:
After 10 years: £100 → £134 (your £100 buys £74 worth)
After 20 years: £100 → £181 (your £100 buys £55 worth)
After 30 years: £100 → £243 (your £100 buys £41 worth)
At 3% inflation, cash loses a quarter of its purchasing power in a decade and over half in 20 years. This isn't a catastrophe — it's the normal, designed operation of a healthy economy. The Bank of England targets 2%. The maths is slow but relentless.
Rule of 72: 72 ÷ inflation rate ≈ years for prices to double. At 3%: 24 years. At 2%: 36 years. See our Compound Interest course for more.

THE RECENT UK EXPERIENCE

Between 2021 and 2024, UK consumer prices rose by over 20%. CPI peaked at 11.1% in October 2022. Even though inflation has fallen back towards 3%, those price increases are permanent.

Common misunderstanding: "Inflation is falling, so prices are coming down." No. Falling inflation means prices are still rising — just less quickly. CPI at 3% means prices rose 3% in the last year. They didn't fall.
Something that cost £100 in January 2021:
Jan 2022: £105.50
Jan 2023: £116.15
Jan 2024: £120.80
Jan 2025: £124.42
Jan 2026: £128.15

Total increase: 28% in 5 years

MODULE 2: REAL VS NOMINAL RETURNS

Your savings account says 4%. Inflation says 3%. The maths says you're growing at 1%.

THE REAL RETURN

Nominal return = the number your bank advertises. Real return = what's left after inflation. The real return is what actually grows your purchasing power.

Approximate: Real return = nominal − inflation
Exact (Fisher): Real = (1 + nominal) / (1 + inflation) − 1

Savings at 4%, inflation 3%:
Real return = 1.04 / 1.03 − 1 = 0.97%
Your bank pays 4%. Inflation runs at 3%. Your real return — the growth in what your money can actually buy — is less than 1%. The headline rate tells you how much more money you'll have. The real rate tells you how much more stuff you can buy.

NEGATIVE REAL RETURNS

2022: Savings ~1.5%, CPI ~9%
Real return: −7.5%

£10,000 in savings:
Nominal end value: £10,150
Purchasing power: £10,150 ÷ 1.09 = £9,312
Real loss: £688
You nominally gained £150 but your money's purchasing power fell by £688. This is the invisible tax of negative real returns.
What to do: If your return is below inflation, you're losing purchasing power. Cash ISAs, index-linked products, and diversified investments all offer different trade-offs. The maths says: below inflation = losing ground.

LONG-TERM IMPACT

£10,000 saved for 20 years:

Nominal 4%, inflation 3% (real 1%):
Purchasing power: £12,124 — real growth: £2,124

Nominal 7%, inflation 3% (real 4%):
Purchasing power: £21,423 — real growth: £11,423
At 1% real, your money barely grows in 20 years. At 4% real, it more than doubles. The gap between a cash account and a diversified portfolio is the difference between treading water and building wealth. But higher returns come with higher risk — no free lunch.

MODULE 3: SHRINKFLATION

When the price stays the same but the product gets smaller.

THE SHRINKFLATION FORMULA

Shrinkflation = smaller product, same price. The unit price rises but the shelf price doesn't change — so most people don't notice.

Effective price increase = (old size / new size) − 1

Crisps: 150g → 130g at £1.50
Old: £10.00/kg → New: £11.54/kg
Effective increase: 15.4%

Chocolate: 200g → 180g at £2.00
Old: £10.00/kg → New: £11.11/kg
Effective increase: 11.1%
A 20g reduction in 200g sounds small. But it's an 11% price increase disguised as a slightly smaller packet. At 3% headline inflation, this product's real price rose nearly four times faster than the official rate.
How to spot it: Always check the unit price (per kg, per litre). UK supermarkets are legally required to display it. The shelf price tells you what you're paying. The unit price tells you what you're getting.
The psychological trick: You're anchored to the shelf price — "it's still £2." The manufacturer knows you won't process a quantity change. They'd rather give you 180g at £2 than 200g at £2.22. Same cost to you. Different perception.
