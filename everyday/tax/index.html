MODULE 1: MARGINAL VS EFFECTIVE TAX RATES

The single biggest misconception in UK tax — and the calculation that fixes it.

THE MYTH

"If I earn over £50,270, all my income gets taxed at 40%."

This is wrong. It's the most common misunderstanding in UK tax, and it causes real harm — people turn down pay rises, refuse overtime, and avoid promotions because they believe crossing a threshold means losing money. The maths says otherwise.

The misconception: Tax bands are flat rates applied to your entire income.
The reality: The UK uses progressive taxation. Your income is sliced into bands, and each band is taxed at a different rate. Only the portion within each band gets that band's rate.

HOW PROGRESSIVE TAXATION WORKS

Think of your income as water filling a series of containers, one on top of the other. The first container (£0–£12,570) isn't taxed at all — that's your Personal Allowance. The second container (£12,571–£50,270) is taxed at 20%. The third (£50,271–£125,140) at 40%. Only the water that overflows into each container gets that container's rate.

The 2025/26 income tax bands for England, Wales, and Northern Ireland:

Band Income range Rate
Personal Allowance £0 – £12,570 0%
Basic rate £12,571 – £50,270 20%
Higher rate £50,271 – £125,140 40%
Additional rate £125,141+ 45%
Key point: These bands haven't moved since 2021. The Personal Allowance is frozen at £12,570 until at least 2028. We'll cover why this matters — and how much it costs you — in Module 5.

WORKED EXAMPLE: £55,000 SALARY

Let's calculate the exact tax bill for someone earning £55,000 in 2025/26.

First £12,570 × 0% = £0.00
Next £37,700 × 20% = £7,540.00
Next £4,730 × 40% = £1,892.00
─────────────────────────────────
Total income tax: £9,432.00
Effective rate: £9,432 ÷ £55,000 = 17.1%
You earn £55,000. Your tax bill is £9,432. That's 17.1% of your total income — not 40%. The 40% rate only applies to the £4,730 that sits above the higher-rate threshold. You keep £45,568 before National Insurance.

NATIONAL INSURANCE — THE SECOND INCOME TAX

National Insurance is calculated separately on the same income. The government calls it something different, but your pay packet doesn't care about the label — it's money taken from your earnings.

2025/26 employee NI rates:

Earnings Rate
Below £12,570/year 0%
£12,571 – £50,270 8%
Above £50,270 2%

Combined marginal rates (income tax + NI) — the real rate on each pound you earn:

£0 – £12,570 → 0% (0% IT + 0% NI)
£12,571 – £50,270 → 28% (20% IT + 8% NI)
£50,271 – £125,140 → 42% (40% IT + 2% NI)
£125,141+ → 47% (45% IT + 2% NI)
For most working people, the real rate is 28p in the pound — 20p income tax plus 8p National Insurance. Cross the higher-rate threshold and it jumps to 42p. The government separates these into "income tax" and "National Insurance" but from your perspective, it's all tax on your earnings.

MODULE 2: THE £100K TRAP

The hidden 60% rate that almost nobody knows exists.

THE PERSONAL ALLOWANCE WITHDRAWAL

The Personal Allowance — that tax-free £12,570 — isn't permanent. Once your income exceeds £100,000, it starts to disappear. For every £2 you earn above £100,000, your Personal Allowance shrinks by £1.

The trap: Between £100,000 and £125,140, you lose your entire Personal Allowance. This creates an effective marginal tax rate of 60–62% — higher than the 45% "additional rate" that the wealthiest pay on income above £125,140.

Here's the mechanics. At £100,000 your Personal Allowance is the full £12,570. At £125,140 it's £0. The withdrawal happens at a rate of £1 lost for every £2 earned.

Personal Allowance at income X (where X > £100,000):

Allowance = £12,570 − ((X − £100,000) ÷ 2)

At £100,000: £12,570 − £0 = £12,570
At £110,000: £12,570 − £5,000 = £7,570
At £120,000: £12,570 − £10,000 = £2,570
At £125,140: £12,570 − £12,570 = £0

THE REAL MARGINAL RATE

When your Personal Allowance shrinks, income that was previously tax-free becomes taxable. This is an additional tax cost on top of the 40% higher rate.

Someone earning £110,000 vs £100,000:

Extra £10,000 earned:
Income tax at 40%: £4,000
NI at 2%: £200
Lost Personal Allowance: £5,000
Tax on that lost £5,000 at 40%: £2,000
─────────────────────────────────
Total tax on extra £10,000: £6,200
Effective marginal rate: 62%
Earn £10,000 more between £100k and £125k, and £6,200 goes to the taxman. You keep £3,800. That's a 62% marginal rate — higher than the 45% "additional rate" that kicks in above £125,140. The £100k–£125k band is the most heavily taxed income in the UK.

WHY THIS MATTERS

Salary sacrifice pensions are extremely valuable in this band. Every pound you put into your pension between £100k and £125k effectively costs you only 38p — because you avoid the 62% marginal rate. This is the single most tax-efficient thing most higher earners can do.

This is also why some employers offer benefits like additional pension contributions instead of pay rises for employees approaching £100k — the tax efficiency is dramatically better.

The maths is clear: If you earn between £100,000 and £125,140, pension contributions, charitable donations (via Gift Aid), or salary sacrifice arrangements are worth examining carefully. They can restore your Personal Allowance and effectively give you a 62% "return" on every pound diverted.

MODULE 3: SCOTLAND — A DIFFERENT SYSTEM

Six bands instead of four. Different thresholds. Same principles.

SCOTTISH INCOME TAX BANDS 2025/26

Scotland has devolved power to set its own income tax rates. The result is a more finely graded system with six bands instead of four. The Personal Allowance remains the same UK-wide (£12,570), and National Insurance is also unchanged.

Band Income range Rate
Personal Allowance£0 – £12,5700%
Starter rate£12,571 – £15,39719%
Basic rate£15,398 – £27,49120%
Intermediate rate£27,492 – £43,66221%
Higher rate£43,663 – £75,00042%
Advanced rate£75,001 – £125,14045%
Top rate£125,141+48%
Scotland's system is more progressive — lower earners pay slightly less, higher earners pay more. The starter rate of 19% (vs 20% in England) means the first few thousand above the Personal Allowance are taxed less. But the higher rate kicks in earlier (£43,663 vs £50,271) and is steeper (42% vs 40%).

SIDE-BY-SIDE: £55,000 SALARY

At £55,000, the difference is roughly £25 per month. The gap widens at higher incomes. A £100,000 earner pays approximately £1,500 more in Scotland than in England per year.
The crossover: Below approximately £28,000, Scottish taxpayers pay slightly less income tax than their English counterparts. Above that, they pay more. The more you earn, the bigger the gap.

MODULE 4: STUDENT LOANS, PENSIONS & MARRIAGE ALLOWANCE

The other deductions on your payslip — and the maths behind each one.

STUDENT LOAN REPAYMENT IS A GRADUATE TAX

Student loan repayment is not like paying off a credit card. It's calculated as a percentage of earnings above a threshold, collected through your payslip, and written off after a set number of years regardless of the balance. In mathematical terms, it behaves exactly like an additional income tax band.

Plan Threshold (2025/26) Rate Written off
Plan 1 (pre-2012)£24,9909%25 years
Plan 2 (2012–2023)£27,2959%30 years
Plan 5 (post-2023)£25,0009%40 years
Postgraduate loan£21,0006%30 years
Plan 2 example — earning £35,000:

Income above threshold: £35,000 − £27,295 = £7,705
Repayment: £7,705 × 9% = £693.45/year
Monthly deduction: £57.79
If you're on Plan 2 earning £35,000, you repay 9% of the £7,705 above the threshold — that's £693 per year, or £58 per month. You don't repay 9% of your whole salary. And if you earn below the threshold, you repay nothing.
The combined marginal rate for a Plan 2 basic-rate taxpayer: 20% income tax + 8% NI + 9% student loan = 37% on every pound between £27,295 and £50,270. For a higher-rate taxpayer with a student loan: 40% + 2% + 9% = 51%.

MARRIAGE ALLOWANCE

If one partner earns less than £12,570 (using none or not all of their Personal Allowance) and the other is a basic-rate taxpayer, you can transfer £1,260 of unused allowance. The receiving partner's tax-free amount increases from £12,570 to £13,830.

Tax saving: £1,260 × 20% = £252/year
It's worth £252 per year. Simple maths, simple claim, but HMRC estimates millions of eligible couples don't claim it. You can also backdate the claim by up to 4 years — that's up to £1,008 in unclaimed tax relief.

PENSION TAX RELIEF

Pension contributions receive tax relief at your marginal rate. The mechanics differ depending on your scheme:

Relief at source (most workplace pensions): You contribute from your net pay, and the pension provider claims 20% basic-rate relief from HMRC automatically. If you're a higher-rate taxpayer, you claim the additional 20% through self-assessment.

Salary sacrifice: Your employer reduces your gross salary and pays the difference into your pension. You save income tax AND National Insurance on the sacrificed amount.

£100 pension contribution — true cost by tax band:

Basic rate (relief at source): £100 − £20 relief = costs you £80
Higher rate (claim via SA): £100 − £40 relief = costs you £60
Additional rate: £100 − £45 relief = costs you £55
£100k–£125k band: £100 − £62 relief = costs you £38

Salary sacrifice (basic rate): saves IT + NI = costs you £72
Every £100 your employer puts into your pension in the £100k–£125k band effectively costs you only £38. That's a 62% "return" before your investments even do anything. There is no other legal tax relief this generous.

MODULE 5: FISCAL DRAG

Why your tax bill goes up even when the rates don't change.

FROZEN THRESHOLDS, RISING WAGES

The Personal Allowance has been frozen at £12,570 since April 2021. The basic-rate threshold has been frozen at £50,270. Both are due to remain frozen until at least 2028.

Meanwhile, average wages rise with inflation. When wages go up but thresholds don't, more of your income gets pushed into higher tax bands. This is called fiscal drag — a stealth tax increase that requires no legislation, no parliamentary vote, and no headline announcement.

Example: Someone earning £50,000 in 2021/22 with 3% annual raises:

2021/22: £50,000 → all within basic rate (threshold: £50,270)
2022/23: £51,500 → £1,230 in higher rate band
2023/24: £53,045 → £2,775 in higher rate band
2024/25: £54,636 → £4,366 in higher rate band
2025/26: £56,275 → £6,005 in higher rate band

Extra tax from fiscal drag (2025/26 vs 2021/22):
£6,005 × (40% − 20%) = £1,201/year
You got a pay rise every year, but the tax thresholds didn't move. Result: you're paying £1,201 more in tax than you would if the thresholds had risen with your wages. The government raised your taxes without changing a single rate.

WHAT YOU CAN DO ABOUT IT

Understanding fiscal drag is the first step to managing it. Pension contributions reduce your taxable income — they can pull you back below a threshold. ISA income is completely outside the tax system. Salary sacrifice arrangements save NI as well as income tax. None of these are "loopholes" — they're the intended mechanisms for managing your effective tax rate.
The bottom line: If your gross salary has risen by 15% since 2021 but the thresholds haven't moved, your effective tax rate has increased even though the rates on paper are identical. The maths doesn't lie, and the maths says threshold freezes are the largest tax increase most working people have experienced in a generation.
