HOW SALARY SACRIFICE WORKS

Salary sacrifice is one of the most tax-efficient ways to boost your pension — but most people don't understand exactly how much they save. Let's fix that with actual numbers.

WHAT SALARY SACRIFICE IS

Salary sacrifice is a formal agreement between you and your employer. You agree to give up part of your gross salary. In return, your employer pays that amount directly into your workplace pension.

The crucial detail: the money never appears on your payslip. As far as HMRC is concerned, your salary is the lower figure. You don't pay income tax on the sacrificed amount. You don't pay National Insurance on it. Your employer doesn't pay employer NI on it either.

This is fundamentally different from making a personal pension contribution. With a personal contribution, your salary stays the same — you pay tax and NI on the full amount, then the pension provider claims back income tax via tax relief. With salary sacrifice, the money is redirected before any deductions happen.

Think of it this way: a personal pension contribution is like paying full price and getting a partial refund. Salary sacrifice is like never being charged in the first place.

WHY IT SAVES MORE MONEY

Let's work through a concrete example. Take a basic rate taxpayer earning £35,000 who wants to put £200 per month (£2,400/year) into their pension.

WITHOUT salary sacrifice (personal contribution): Gross salary: £35,000 Income tax (20%): -£4,486 Employee NI (8%): -£1,794 Take-home before pension: £28,720 Personal pension contribution: -£2,400 Take-home after pension: £26,320 Pension receives: £2,400 + £600 tax relief = £3,000
WITH salary sacrifice: Gross salary (reduced): £32,600 Income tax (20%): -£4,006 Employee NI (8%): -£1,602 Take-home: £26,992 Pension receives: £2,400 (directly from employer)

Compare the two outcomes:

PERSONAL CONTRIBUTION
Your take-home£26,320
Pension receives£3,000
NI paid by you£1,794
SALARY SACRIFICE
Your take-home£26,992
Pension receives£2,400
NI paid by you£1,602

With salary sacrifice, your take-home is £672 higher and your pension still receives a direct £2,400. The NI saving is £192/year — money that would have been lost entirely with a personal contribution. Many employers also pass on their own employer NI saving (13.8% of the sacrificed amount = £331/year), which goes straight into your pension pot.

If your employer passes on their NI saving, your pension receives £2,400 + £331 = £2,731. You keep £672 more in take-home. The total benefit versus a personal contribution: £1,003 per year — from sacrificing the same £200/month.

HIGHER RATE AND ADDITIONAL RATE

The savings become dramatically larger at higher tax rates. Same £200/month sacrifice:

HIGHER RATE TAXPAYER (£60,000 salary, 40% tax + 2% NI): Tax saving on £2,400: £960 (vs £480 via tax relief reclaim) NI saving: £48 Your annual benefit: £1,008 Employer NI saving: £331 (13.8% of £2,400) Cost to your take-home: only £1,392 for £2,400 in pension Every £1 sacrificed costs you just 58p.
ADDITIONAL RATE TAXPAYER (£160,000 salary, 45% tax + 2% NI): Tax saving on £2,400: £1,080 NI saving: £48 Your annual benefit: £1,128 Employer NI saving: £331 Cost to your take-home: only £1,272 for £2,400 in pension Every £1 sacrificed costs you just 53p.

And there is a particularly powerful zone between £100,000 and £125,140 where your personal allowance is tapered away. In this band, the effective marginal rate is 60%+. Salary sacrifice here saves you more than 60p for every £1 redirected. If your salary sits in this range, salary sacrifice is one of the most efficient moves available in the entire UK tax system.

THE CATCHES

Salary sacrifice is not free money. There are real trade-offs you need to understand before committing.

Reduced official salary. Your contractual gross salary drops. Mortgage lenders, landlords, and credit providers see the lower figure. If you're planning a mortgage application in the next 6-12 months, consider pausing salary sacrifice or asking your employer for a letter confirming your pre-sacrifice salary. Not all lenders accept these.
Lower statutory payments. Statutory maternity pay (SMP), statutory paternity pay (SPP), and statutory sick pay (SSP) are all calculated on your reduced salary. If you're planning to start a family, check whether your employer offers enhanced maternity/paternity pay — if they do, it may be based on the pre-sacrifice figure, but you need to confirm this.
State Pension risk for low earners. If salary sacrifice takes your earnings below the NI Lower Earnings Limit (£6,396 in 2025/26), you stop accruing qualifying years for the State Pension. This is unlikely for full-time workers but is a genuine risk for part-time employees making large sacrifices.
Reduced death-in-service benefit. If your employer provides life insurance as a multiple of salary (e.g. 4x salary), salary sacrifice reduces the payout. A £50,000 salary sacrificing £5,000 means your death-in-service benefit drops from £200,000 to £180,000.

STUDENT LOAN INTERACTION

Here is an often-overlooked benefit: salary sacrifice reduces your student loan repayments. Loan repayments are calculated on your gross salary — and after sacrifice, that gross salary is lower.

Plan 2 student loan (most graduates since 2012): Repayment: 9% of everything above £27,295 Without sacrifice (£35,000 salary): Repayable income: £35,000 - £27,295 = £7,705 Annual repayment: £7,705 × 9% = £693.45 With £200/month sacrifice (£32,600 salary): Repayable income: £32,600 - £27,295 = £5,305 Annual repayment: £5,305 × 9% = £477.45 Student loan saving: £216/year

Every £1,000 you sacrifice saves £90 per year in Plan 2 loan repayments. That's money redirected from a loan charging RPI + 3% interest to a pension that should be growing at 5-8% long-term. Mathematically, this is a no-brainer for most graduates — especially those who are unlikely to fully repay their loan before the 30-year write-off.

If you have a Plan 2 student loan AND you're a basic rate taxpayer, salary sacrifice saves you 20% income tax + 8% NI + 9% student loan = 37% on every pound sacrificed. Your pension receives £1 for every 63p it costs your take-home.

The same logic applies to Plan 1 (9% above £24,990), Plan 4 (9% above £31,395), Plan 5 (9% above £25,000), and postgraduate loans (6% above £21,000). If you're on multiple plans, the savings stack.

WHAT TO DO NEXT

Salary sacrifice is not something you can set up unilaterally — it requires your employer's agreement and a formal variation to your employment contract. Here's the practical sequence:

1. Use our Salary Sacrifice Calculator to see your exact tax, NI, and student loan savings at your specific salary.

2. Ask your HR department whether salary sacrifice is available and whether they pass on the employer NI saving to your pension.

3. Check whether you have any upcoming mortgage applications, maternity/paternity leave, or other events where your contractual salary matters.

4. If everything checks out, the maths almost always favours sacrifice over personal contributions — and the higher your tax rate, the larger the advantage.
