HOW TO CALCULATE POT ODDS

Every poker decision is a maths problem. You do not need to be good at maths to solve it — you need to understand one ratio. That ratio is called pot odds, and once you can calculate it, you will never again have to guess whether calling a bet is profitable.

WHAT ARE POT ODDS?

Pot odds compare the size of the bet you must call to the size of the pot you stand to win. They answer one question: is the price right?

If you are getting good enough odds from the pot relative to your chance of making your hand, the call is mathematically correct — regardless of whether you win this particular hand.

Pot odds are not about predicting the future. They are about making decisions that are profitable over hundreds of hands. You will lose individual hands. You will win the war.

THE FORMULA

Pot Odds = Amount to Call ÷ (Current Pot + Amount to Call)
In plain English: what fraction of the total pot am I putting in? If I am putting in a small fraction but could win the whole thing, the price is good. If I am putting in a large fraction, the price is bad — unless my hand is very likely to improve.

A WORKED EXAMPLE

You hold 9♠ 8♠. The board is 7♠ 2♠ K♦. You have a flush draw — any spade completes your hand.

The pot is £40. Your opponent bets £10. Should you call?

STEP 1: CALCULATE YOUR POT ODDS

Pot = £40 (existing) + £10 (opponent's bet) = £50 total Cost to call = £10 Pot odds = £10 ÷ (£50 + £10) = £10 ÷ £60 = 0.167 = 16.7%
You are putting in £10 to win a pot of £60. That means you need to win this hand more than 16.7% of the time to break even. Anything above that is profit.

STEP 2: CALCULATE YOUR CHANCE OF HITTING

You have a flush draw. There are 13 spades in a deck, you can see 4 of them (two in your hand, two on the board), so there are 9 spades left. You have seen 5 cards total (2 in hand + 3 on board), leaving 47 unseen.

Outs = 9 (remaining spades) Cards unseen = 47 Chance of hitting on next card = 9 ÷ 47 = 19.1%
You have roughly a 19% chance of completing your flush on the turn. Your pot odds require only 16.7%. Since 19.1% > 16.7%, this is a profitable call.

STEP 3: DECIDE

Your equity (19.1%) exceeds the price you are paying (16.7%). Call. Over thousands of hands, this exact situation will make you money — even though you will miss the flush roughly 4 times out of 5.

THE RULE OF 2 AND 4

Calculating exact percentages at the table is slow. There is a shortcut that is accurate enough for every real-world decision.

After the flop (two cards to come): Outs × 4 = approximate % chance After the turn (one card to come): Outs × 2 = approximate % chance
With 9 outs after the flop: 9 × 4 = 36%. With 9 outs after the turn: 9 × 2 = 18%. Compare these to the exact figures — 35.0% and 19.1% respectively — and you can see the shortcut is remarkably close.

CONVERTING BETWEEN RATIOS AND PERCENTAGES

Some players think in ratios (3:1, 4:1). Some think in percentages (25%, 20%). You should be comfortable with both.

Ratio to percentage: 3:1 means you call 1 out of every 4 units = 1 ÷ 4 = 25% 4:1 means you call 1 out of every 5 units = 1 ÷ 5 = 20% Percentage to ratio: 25% = 1 in 4 = 3:1 (three parts pot, one part your call) 20% = 1 in 5 = 4:1
If the pot is £30 and you must call £10, you are getting 3:1 (£30 to your £10). As a percentage: £10 ÷ £40 total = 25%. You need to win more than 25% of the time.

IMPLIED ODDS

Pot odds only account for money already in the pot. But poker hands have more betting rounds to come. Implied odds estimate the additional money you expect to win on later streets if you hit your hand.

Implied odds matter most when you have a disguised draw. If you hit a flush and your opponent cannot see it coming, they are likely to pay you off on the river. That extra money changes the equation.

EXAMPLE

Pot = £30. Opponent bets £15. Cost to call = £15. Pot odds = £15 ÷ £60 = 25%. You need 25% equity. Your flush draw gives you 19% on the next card alone. Strict pot odds say fold. But if you expect your opponent to call a £30 river bet when you hit: Implied pot = £60 (current) + £30 (expected river call) = £90 Implied odds = £15 ÷ £105 = 14.3% Now you only need 14.3%. Your 19% equity clears that easily.
Implied odds make borderline calls profitable by accounting for future money. But be honest with yourself — implied odds only work if your opponent actually pays you when you hit. Against tight players who fold to aggression, your implied odds are lower than you think.
Implied odds are an estimate, not a guarantee. The most common mistake is overestimating how much your opponent will pay when you hit. Be conservative. If you are routinely using implied odds to justify calls, you are probably calling too much.

REVERSE IMPLIED ODDS

Sometimes you hit your hand and still lose. This happens when your draw completes to a second-best hand — for example, making a low flush when your opponent has a higher flush, or hitting a straight on a paired board where a full house is possible.

Reverse implied odds are highest when your draw is obvious (everyone can see the flush came in), when you could be drawing to a second-best hand, or when your opponent's range includes hands that beat your completed draw.

WHEN POT ODDS DO NOT APPLY

Pot odds assume you can see the hand to completion. There are situations where the framework breaks down.

Tournament play with ICM pressure. In tournaments, chips have non-linear value. Risking your entire stack on a mathematically correct call might still be wrong if busting costs you more expected value than the pot is worth. Pot odds are a cash game tool first.

Tiny edges against large bets. If your pot odds give you a 1% edge but you must risk your entire stack, variance will destroy you before the long run arrives. Bankroll management overrides marginal pot odds.

Multiway pots. When three or more players are in the hand, pot odds change because other players may bet or raise behind you. Your effective odds are harder to calculate.

PUTTING IT ALL TOGETHER

Here is the complete decision process, every hand, every time:

1. Count outs (how many cards help you) 2. Estimate equity (outs × 2 after turn, outs × 4 after flop) 3. Calculate pot odds (call ÷ total pot including your call) 4. Compare: equity > pot odds → call. Equity < pot odds → fold. 5. Adjust for implied odds if there are streets remaining
That is five steps. In practice, it takes seconds. Count your outs, multiply by 2 or 4, compare to what the pot is offering you. The answer is either yes or no. Poker becomes much simpler when you stop guessing and start calculating.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

What are pot odds in poker?

Pot odds compare the cost of calling a bet to the total pot size, expressed as a percentage. They tell you the minimum win rate needed to make a call profitable over time.

How do you calculate pot odds quickly?

Divide the amount you must call by the total pot (including your call). For example, calling £10 into a £50 pot: £10 ÷ £60 = 16.7%. You need to win more than 16.7% of the time.

What is the Rule of 2 and 4?

A shortcut for estimating hand equity. After the flop, multiply your outs by 4. After the turn, multiply by 2. With 9 outs after the flop: 9 × 4 = 36% chance of completing your hand.

What are implied odds?

Implied odds account for money you expect to win on future betting rounds if you hit your draw. They make borderline calls profitable when opponents are likely to pay off your completed hand.

Should I always call if pot odds are in my favour?

In cash games, generally yes — that is the mathematically correct play over time. In tournaments, ICM pressure and stack considerations can make a mathematically correct call strategically wrong.
