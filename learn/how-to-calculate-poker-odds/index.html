HOW TO CALCULATE POKER ODDS

Every poker decision reduces to a single question: is the price right? This guide teaches you the mathematics to answer it.

Poker is not a game of luck. Over any meaningful sample, the player who makes mathematically correct decisions wins. The player who relies on gut feeling donates money to those who don't.

This guide covers the core mathematics you need: probability, outs, the Rule of 2 and 4, pot odds, and expected value. No prior maths knowledge required. Every concept is built from first principles with worked examples.

If you can multiply, divide, and compare two numbers, you can calculate poker odds. That's it. Everything else is vocabulary.

PROBABILITY BASICS — CARDS AND COMBINATIONS

A standard deck has 52 cards: 13 ranks (2 through Ace) in each of 4 suits (spades, hearts, diamonds, clubs). Before any cards are dealt, every card is equally likely. The probability of any specific card being dealt is:

P(specific card) = 1/52 = 0.0192 = 1.92%

In Texas Hold'em, you see your 2 hole cards, then the board is dealt: 3 cards on the flop, 1 on the turn, 1 on the river. After you see your hole cards, there are 50 unknown cards remaining. After the flop, there are 47 unknown cards. After the turn, 46.

Probability in poker is always about counting: how many of the unseen cards help you, divided by how many unseen cards remain.

P(hitting your card) = cards that help you / unseen cards remaining
This is the foundation of every poker calculation. All the techniques below are shortcuts for this single fraction.

COUNTING OUTS

An out is any unseen card that completes your draw and (presumably) gives you the winning hand. Counting outs is the first step in every poker odds calculation.

Here are the common draws and their out counts:

DrawOutsExample
Flush draw94 suited cards, 13 - 4 = 9 remaining
Open-ended straight draw85-6-7-8 on board, need a 4 or 9
Gutshot straight draw45-6-_-8 on board, need only a 7
Two overcards6A-K vs lower pair, 3 Aces + 3 Kings

Flush draw + open-ended straight159 flush outs + 6 non-flush straight outs
Be careful not to double-count. If a card completes both your flush and your straight, it's still only one out. That's why the combo draws above have fewer outs than flush + straight added together.

Also beware of tainted outs. If one of your "outs" also completes a better hand for your opponent (e.g. it puts a fourth flush card on the board when your opponent may already have the flush), discount it. Experienced players routinely shave 1-2 outs from their count to account for this.

THE RULE OF 2 AND 4

The Rule of 2 and 4 is a mental shortcut that converts outs into an approximate percentage chance of hitting.

After the flop (2 cards to come): Outs x 4 = approximate % equity After the turn (1 card to come): Outs x 2 = approximate % equity

Why does this work? After the flop, you have two chances to hit your out (turn and river). With 47 unseen cards, each out gives you roughly 1/47 per card = about 2.1% per card, times 2 remaining cards = about 4.2%. The "times 4" approximation is close enough for table decisions.

Here's how the estimate compares to the exact probability for common draws:

The Rule of 4 slightly overestimates with large out counts (12+). With 15 outs, it says 60% when the real figure is 54%. For everyday table decisions with 4-9 outs, the approximation is excellent. If you want a tighter estimate for big draws, use outs x 4 minus (outs - 8) when outs exceed 8.

POT ODDS

Pot odds tell you how much equity you need to justify calling a bet. The formula is:

Pot odds = Cost to call / (Pot + Opponent's bet + Your call) Or equivalently: Pot odds = Cost to call / Total pot after your call

WORKED EXAMPLE

The pot is £30. Your opponent bets £10. You must call £10.

Total pot after your call = £30 + £10 + £10 = £50 Pot odds = £10 / £50 = 0.20 = 20%

You need more than 20% equity to call profitably.

Suppose you have a flush draw (9 outs). After the flop, the Rule of 4 gives you approximately 36% equity. Since 36% > 20%, calling is mathematically correct. You'll lose more often than you win, but when you hit the flush, the pot more than compensates.

The decision rule is simple: if your equity is greater than your pot odds, call. If it's less, fold. If it's roughly equal, it's a marginal spot where other factors (position, opponent tendencies, implied odds) tip the balance.

Memorise these. When someone bets half the pot, you need 25% equity. A flush draw after the flop gives you 35%. You don't need a calculator for this at the table once you know the numbers.

EXPECTED VALUE

Expected value (EV) puts a monetary figure on a decision. It answers the question: if I made this exact play a thousand times, how much would I gain or lose on average per hand?

EV = (P(win) x Amount won) - (P(lose) x Amount lost)

WORKED EXAMPLE — FLUSH DRAW FACING A BET

You have a flush draw on the flop. The pot is £60. Your opponent bets £20. You must call £20 to continue.

Equity (flush draw, 2 cards to come) = 35% If you call and hit (35% of the time): You win the pot = £60 + £20 (their bet) + £20 (your call) = £100 total Your profit = £100 - £20 (your investment) = £80 If you call and miss (65% of the time): You lose your £20 call EV(call) = (0.35 x £80) - (0.65 x £20) = £28.00 - £13.00 = +£15.00

The expected value of calling is +£15.00. This means that every time you face this exact situation and call, you profit £15 on average. You'll miss the flush more often than you hit it, but the pot is offering you a price that more than compensates for the risk.

A positive EV decision is correct even when it loses. You'll miss the flush 65% of the time. But making +EV calls consistently, over hundreds of hands, is where your profit accumulates. That's the maths always winning.

For comparison, let's check the EV of folding:

EV(fold) = £0 Folding is never wrong in the sense that you don't lose anything — but you forfeit +£15.00 of expected value every time you fold here. Over 100 identical spots, that's £1,500 left on the table.

IMPLIED ODDS

Strict pot odds assume no further betting. But poker has multiple streets. If you hit your draw, you'll often win additional bets from your opponent on later streets. This extra money is called implied odds.

Implied odds can justify a call even when pot odds alone say fold. If the pot is offering you 22% but you need 25%, the missing 3% might be covered by the additional bets you'll extract when you hit.

Factors that increase implied odds:

Your opponent has a strong but second-best hand (e.g. top pair when you're drawing to a flush) and will pay you off. You are in position, giving you control of the final betting round. Your draw is hidden — a backdoor flush or runner-runner straight that your opponent won't see coming.

Factors that decrease implied odds:

The board is obviously dangerous (three to a flush, four to a straight) and your opponent will shut down if you hit. Your opponent is short-stacked and doesn't have enough money behind to pay you. You are out of position and may not get the chance to extract value.

Implied odds are real, but they are the most commonly overestimated factor in amateur poker. Beginners routinely justify loose calls with "implied odds" when their opponent has no intention of putting another penny in. If you can't articulate specifically why your opponent will pay you off, you don't have implied odds — you have wishful thinking.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

What is the Rule of 2 and 4 in poker?

After the flop, multiply your outs by 4 to estimate your percentage chance of hitting by the river. After the turn, multiply by 2. For example, a flush draw has 9 outs: 9 x 4 = 36% after the flop, 9 x 2 = 18% after the turn. These are approximations — the exact figures are 35.0% and 19.6%.

How do you calculate pot odds in poker?

Pot odds = cost to call divided by (current pot + opponent's bet + your call). If the pot is £30, your opponent bets £10, and you must call £10, your pot odds are £10 / £50 = 20%. If your equity exceeds 20%, calling is mathematically correct.

How many outs does a flush draw have?

A flush draw has 9 outs. You hold 2 cards of one suit and the board shows 2 more, so 4 of the 13 cards in that suit are accounted for. That leaves 13 minus 4 = 9 unseen cards that complete your flush.

What is expected value (EV) in poker?

Expected value is the average amount you win or lose on a decision over the long run. EV = (probability of winning x amount won) minus (probability of losing x amount lost). A positive EV decision is profitable over time, even if you lose on any individual hand.
