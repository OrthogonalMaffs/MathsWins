HOW BOOKMAKER ODDS WORK

Every price a bookmaker offers is worse than the true probability. The gap between what they offer and what is fair has a name — and understanding it changes how you think about every bet you place.

THE PRICE IS NEVER FAIR

Walk into any bookmaker or open any betting app, and every single price you see has been adjusted in the house's favour. The odds on offer do not reflect the true probability of an outcome — they reflect a probability that has been deliberately inflated. The bookmaker's profit is baked into every market before a single pound is wagered.

This built-in margin is called the overround (also known as vig or juice in the US). It is the single most important concept in betting mathematics, and most recreational bettors have never heard of it. Once you understand it, you will never look at a set of odds the same way again.

FROM PROBABILITY TO ODDS

Before we can see the overround, we need to understand how probability becomes odds. The conversion is straightforward: divide 1 by the probability to get the fair decimal odds.

Take the simplest possible event — a fair coin toss. Heads has a 50% chance of landing. Tails has a 50% chance. If a bookmaker offered fair odds on this event, both outcomes would pay 2.00 in decimal odds.

Fair odds = 1 ÷ probability Heads: 1 ÷ 0.50 = 2.00 Tails: 1 ÷ 0.50 = 2.00

This is a 100% book. The implied probabilities sum to exactly 100%: you convert each decimal price back to a probability using 1 ÷ odds × 100, and if they add up to 100%, the market is perfectly fair. There is no margin for the house. No bookmaker in the real world operates this way.

HOW BOOKMAKERS ADJUST THE ODDS

Instead of offering 2.00 on both sides of that coin toss, a bookmaker might offer 1.91 on heads and 1.91 on tails. The payout looks similar, but the mathematics have shifted fundamentally.

Bookmaker's coin toss market: Heads 1.91 → implied prob = 1/1.91 = 52.36% Tails 1.91 → implied prob = 1/1.91 = 52.36% Total implied probability = 104.7% Overround = 104.7% − 100% = 4.7%

The bookmaker has created a market where the implied probabilities sum to 104.7%. That extra 4.7% is the overround. It means that for every £100 wagered proportionally across all outcomes, the bookmaker expects to pay out roughly £95.50 and keep £4.50. The margin is invisible to most punters because it is spread across every price in the market rather than charged as an explicit fee.

The overround is the betting equivalent of the house edge in a casino. A European roulette wheel has a 2.7% house edge. A football match-result market at 5% overround has a roughly comparable cost per bet. The difference is that casino edges are fixed by the rules of the game, while bookmaker margins vary wildly between markets, events, and operators.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLES

The overround varies enormously depending on the type of market. Here are three worked examples that show the range you will encounter in practice.

FOOTBALL MATCH RESULT (1X2)

A Premier League fixture with three outcomes — home win, draw, away win. This is typically one of the most competitive markets available.

Arsenal vs Chelsea Home 1.95 → 1/1.95 = 51.28% Draw 3.60 → 1/3.60 = 27.78% Away 4.00 → 1/4.00 = 25.00% Total = 104.06% Overround = 4.06%

At 4.06%, this is a competitive market. You are paying roughly 4p per pound in margin. Over a season of bets, this adds up, but it is manageable — especially if you shop around for the best price on your specific selection.

HORSE RACING — 10 RUNNERS

Horse racing markets have more outcomes, which gives bookmakers more room to build in margin across a wider spread of prices.

10-runner handicap: Horse 1: 3.50 → 28.57% Horse 6: 11.00 → 9.09% Horse 2: 5.00 → 20.00% Horse 7: 13.00 → 7.69% Horse 3: 6.00 → 16.67% Horse 8: 17.00 → 5.88% Horse 4: 7.00 → 14.29% Horse 9: 21.00 → 4.76% Horse 5: 9.00 → 11.11% Horse 10: 34.00 → 2.94% Total = 121.00% Overround = 21.00%

At 21%, this is a very different proposition from football. For every £100 staked proportionally, the bookmaker expects to keep £17.36. Most of the margin is hidden in the shorter-priced runners, where a small odds reduction translates to a large probability shift.

CORRECT SCORE

Correct score markets typically offer 15–25 possible outcomes. This is where bookmakers extract their largest margins, and where most punters pay the least attention to value.

Correct score market (selected prices): 1-0: 7.00 → 14.29% 0-1: 9.00 → 11.11% 2-1: 9.50 → 10.53% 1-2: 12.00 → 8.33% 0-0: 8.00 → 12.50% 2-0: 10.00 → 10.00% 1-1: 7.50 → 13.33% Other: 5.50 → 18.18% ...plus 12 more outcomes Typical total = 130–145% Overround = 30–45%
The pattern is clear: more outcomes means higher hidden overround. With only 3 outcomes, a bookmaker has limited room to build margin without the prices looking obviously poor. With 20+ outcomes, the margin can be spread so thinly across each selection that no individual price looks unreasonable — but the total cost to the bettor is enormous.

ACCUMULATORS — WHERE OVERROUND COMPOUNDS

Single bets are expensive enough. Accumulators are where the mathematics become truly punitive, and it is no coincidence that bookmakers promote them more aggressively than any other product.

The overround on an accumulator does not simply add up. It multiplies. Each leg compounds the bookmaker's edge, just as compound interest grows a savings balance — except here, the compounding works against you.

Combined edge = (book₁ × book₂ × ... × bookₙ) × 100 − 100 Where each book = total implied probability ÷ 100 Example: 4-leg accumulator, each market at 5% overround Each book = 1.05 Combined = 1.05 × 1.05 × 1.05 × 1.05 = 1.2155 Combined edge = 121.55% − 100% = 21.6%

Four legs at 5% overround each gives the bookmaker a 21.6% combined edge. Not 20%. The compounding effect adds an extra 1.6 percentage points that most bettors never account for.

The damage escalates rapidly with more legs:

Accumulator edges at 5% overround per leg: 2 legs: 10.3% edge £0.907 return per £1 3 legs: 15.8% edge £0.864 return per £1 4 legs: 21.6% edge £0.823 return per £1 5 legs: 27.6% edge £0.784 return per £1 7 legs: 40.7% edge £0.711 return per £1 10 legs: 62.9% edge £0.614 return per £1
A 10-leg accumulator at just 5% overround per leg gives the bookmaker a 62.9% edge. You are expected to lose 63p of every £1 staked. For comparison, a roulette wheel has a 2.7% house edge and a slot machine typically runs at 3–8%. The 10-fold acca is mathematically worse than almost any game in the casino. Bookmakers promote accumulators for a reason — they are the single most profitable product on the menu.

WHAT YOU CAN DO ABOUT IT

Overround is a fact of betting — you cannot eliminate it entirely. But you can reduce its impact significantly. Here are the approaches that actually move the needle.

Compare bookmakers on every bet. Two bookmakers might both offer your selection at 2.10, but if one runs a 103% book and the other runs a 108% book, the first is giving you meaningfully better value. Price comparison is the single easiest way to reduce the margin you pay. Over hundreds of bets, the difference between 3% and 8% overround is the difference between slow losses and near break-even.
Use betting exchanges. Platforms like Betfair and Smarkets match bettors against each other rather than against a bookmaker. Their markets typically run at 100–102% overround. You pay a commission on winning bets (usually 2–5%), but the total cost is almost always lower than traditional bookmaker margin. If you bet regularly and are not using exchanges, you are overpaying on virtually every wager.
Avoid high-overround markets. Correct score, first goalscorer, anytime scorer, and novelty markets routinely carry overrounds above 20%. These are the bookmaker's highest-margin products. If you want the fairest prices, stick to match results, Asian handicaps, and totals (over/under) markets, where competition between bookmakers keeps margins tight.
Keep accumulators short. If you enjoy accumulators, limit yourself to 2 or 3 legs. Beyond that, the compounding overround creates an edge that is almost impossible to overcome through selection skill alone. A 3-leg acca at 5% overround costs you 15.8%. A 10-leg acca at the same overround costs you 62.9%. The maths is unambiguous.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

What is overround in betting?

Overround is the bookmaker's built-in profit margin. It is the amount by which the implied probabilities of all outcomes in a market exceed 100%. For example, if you convert every price in a football match to implied probabilities and they sum to 105%, the overround is 5%. The terms “vig” (short for vigorish) and “juice” mean the same thing — they are the American equivalents.

Why do bookmaker odds always add up to more than 100%?

Because the bookmaker needs a profit margin. Fair odds would sum to exactly 100%, but that would leave no room for the bookmaker to cover costs or generate income. By shortening every price slightly, the bookmaker ensures that the implied probabilities exceed 100%. The excess is their guaranteed edge, regardless of which outcome wins.

How does overround affect accumulators?

Overround compounds with each leg. It multiplies rather than adds. If each market has 5% overround, a 4-leg accumulator gives the bookmaker a 21.6% combined edge (not 20%). A 10-leg accumulator at the same per-leg overround produces a 62.9% edge — worse than virtually any casino game. This compounding effect is the primary mathematical reason why long accumulators are so profitable for bookmakers.

How can I reduce the impact of overround on my bets?

Four approaches make a real difference. First, compare prices across multiple bookmakers — even small differences in overround add up over hundreds of bets. Second, use betting exchanges (Betfair, Smarkets), which typically run at 100–102% instead of 104–110%. Third, avoid exotic markets like correct score and first goalscorer, where overround often exceeds 20%. Fourth, keep accumulators to 2–3 legs maximum to limit the compounding effect.
