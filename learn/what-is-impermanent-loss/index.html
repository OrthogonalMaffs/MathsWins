WHAT IS IMPERMANENT LOSS?

Providing liquidity to a decentralised exchange looks straightforward — deposit two tokens, earn trading fees. What most liquidity providers discover too late is that the mechanism designed to keep prices accurate is also silently eroding their position.

THE MECHANISM NOBODY EXPLAINS

Decentralised exchanges (DEXs) like Uniswap do not use an order book. There are no buyers and sellers matching up at an agreed price. Instead, they use an automated market maker (AMM) — a mathematical formula that sets prices based on the ratio of tokens in a liquidity pool.

The formula is simple:

x × y = k Where x is the quantity of token A in the pool, y is the quantity of token B, and k is a constant that never changes.

When a trader buys token A from the pool, they add token B and remove token A. The ratio shifts. The formula ensures the product stays constant, which means the price of token A rises as it becomes scarcer in the pool.

This mechanism keeps prices aligned with the broader market through arbitrage. If the price of token A rises on a centralised exchange, arbitrageurs buy it cheaply from the DEX pool until the DEX price matches. This arbitrage is automatic and relentless — and it is the source of impermanent loss.

WHAT IMPERMANENT LOSS ACTUALLY IS

When you deposit into a liquidity pool, you deposit equal value of both tokens. As the price of one token changes relative to the other, the AMM rebalances the pool through arbitrage. You end up holding more of the token that fell in price and less of the token that rose.

Compare this to simply holding the same tokens in a wallet — a strategy called HODLing. Impermanent loss is the difference between what your position is worth inside the pool versus what it would be worth had you simply held.

Worked example — ETH/USDC pool: Deposit: 1 ETH + 1,000 USDC when ETH = £1,000 Pool value at deposit: £2,000 k = 1 × 1,000 = 1,000 ETH price doubles to £2,000. New pool ratio (solving x × y = 1,000 with y/x = 2,000): x = √(1,000/2,000) = 0.7071 ETH y = √(1,000 × 2,000) = 1,414.2 USDC Pool value: (0.7071 × £2,000) + £1,414.2 = £2,828.4 HODL value: (1 × £2,000) + £1,000 = £3,000 Impermanent loss = £3,000 − £2,828.4 = £171.60 Loss as % of HODL value = 5.72%
You gained from the price rise — your pool position is worth £2,828 instead of £2,000. But a HODLer gained more: £3,000. The difference — £171.60 — is the impermanent loss. You paid it invisibly, in the form of selling ETH to arbitrageurs as the price rose.

HOW LOSS SCALES WITH PRICE CHANGE

Impermanent loss grows with the magnitude of price divergence, not the direction. A token that halves causes the same impermanent loss as a token that doubles, because the AMM rebalances symmetrically.

Impermanent loss by price change (one token vs the other): Price change → IL as % of HODL value 1.25× → 0.6% 1.50× → 2.0% 2× → 5.7% 3× → 13.4% 4× → 20.0% 5× → 25.5% 10× → 42.5%
At a 2× price change, you lose 5.7% compared to holding. At 5×, you lose 25.5%. At 10×, you lose 42.5% of what you would have had by simply holding both assets.

WHY “IMPERMANENT” IS A GENEROUS DESCRIPTION

The loss is impermanent only in a narrow mathematical sense. In practice, it becomes permanent the moment you withdraw. Three scenarios:

Price returns to entry ratio. Impermanent loss goes to zero. You keep all the trading fees you earned. This is the best case, and the least common.
Price partially recovers. You withdraw with some impermanent loss remaining, partially offset by fees earned. Whether you are ahead depends on fee income versus loss magnitude.
Price divergence widens further or never recovers. You withdraw with significant impermanent loss. The trading fees may or may not cover it. For volatile token pairs in low-volume pools, they often do not.
The word “impermanent” describes a mathematical property of the mechanism, not a reassurance about your outcome. Many liquidity providers who lose money do so because they misread “impermanent” as “temporary and therefore ignorable.”

WHEN FEES COVER THE LOSS — AND WHEN THEY DON’T

Impermanent loss is not automatically a reason to avoid liquidity provision. Trading fees can offset it — and in high-volume pools, they often do. The question is always whether the fees earned exceed the impermanent loss incurred.

Fee income depends on: trading volume in the pool, fee tier (typically 0.05%, 0.30%, or 1.00% per trade), your share of the pool.

Rough framework: Low IL risk (stablecoin pairs, pegged pairs): Fee income likely exceeds IL Medium IL risk (ETH/BTC, blue-chip pairs): Fee income may cover IL in high-volume pools High IL risk (ETH/small-cap altcoin): IL frequently exceeds fee income Requires careful monitoring

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

What is impermanent loss in crypto?

Impermanent loss is the difference between holding tokens in a liquidity pool versus simply holding them in a wallet. When token prices diverge, the AMM rebalances your position — you end up with more of the cheaper token and less of the expensive one, resulting in a lower total value compared to holding.

Why does impermanent loss happen?

It happens because of the constant product formula (x × y = k) used by automated market makers. When prices change, arbitrageurs trade against the pool to restore alignment with external markets. This rebalancing extracts value from liquidity providers.

Is impermanent loss permanent?

The loss is “impermanent” only if prices return to the exact ratio at which you deposited. If you withdraw at any other ratio, the loss is realised and permanent. In practice, prices rarely return to exact entry ratios.

Can trading fees cover impermanent loss?

Sometimes. High-volume pools with stable pairs (like stablecoins) often generate enough fees to exceed impermanent loss. Volatile pairs with low volume frequently do not — the IL outpaces fee income.
