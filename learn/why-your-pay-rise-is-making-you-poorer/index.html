WHY YOUR PAY RISE IS MAKING YOU POORER

You received a pay rise. Your payslip went up. And yet, at the end of every month, there is less room in your budget than there was five years ago. This is not poor financial management. It is the predictable outcome of two mechanisms working simultaneously on your income — and most people have never had either explained clearly.

THE TWO MECHANISMS

The first is inflation. When prices rise faster than your salary, your pay buys less than it did before, even if the number on your payslip has increased. A 5% pay rise in a year of 10% inflation is not a pay rise — it is a real-terms pay cut of approximately 4.5%.

The second is fiscal drag, also called bracket creep. When the government freezes the income thresholds at which higher tax rates apply, and salaries rise even modestly, more of your income falls into higher tax brackets automatically. No vote is required. No announcement is made. Your tax bill rises silently, year after year, purely because a threshold did not move while your salary did.

Together, these mechanisms explain why millions of experienced public sector workers — nurses at the top of their pay band, teachers at the top of the main pay scale — feel considerably poorer than they did in 2019, despite receiving pay rises in most intervening years.

UNDERSTANDING INFLATION

Inflation measures the rate at which prices rise across the economy. The UK’s headline measure is the Consumer Prices Index (CPI), published monthly by the Office for National Statistics. It tracks the cost of a representative basket of goods and services — food, energy, transport, housing, clothing — and calculates how much more expensive that basket has become over the past twelve months.

When CPI is 10%, the same basket that cost £1,000 last year costs £1,100 this year. Your money buys 9.1% less than it did.

Real wage growth = Nominal pay rise − Inflation rate (approximate) If your salary rises 5% and inflation is 10%: Real wage change ≈ 5% − 10% = −5% In real terms — in terms of actual purchasing power — you are 5% worse off. The number on your payslip went up. The amount your life costs went up more.
UK CPI annual rates (ONS): 2019/20: 1.5% 2020/21: 0.7% 2021/22: 5.4% 2022/23: 10.1% 2023/24: 6.7% 2024/25: ~2.5% Cumulative price rise 2019 to 2024: approximately 26%
That 26% is the benchmark. Any worker whose total salary increase over the same period falls short of 26% has experienced a real-terms pay cut, regardless of how many individual pay rises they received.

THE PAY SCALE CEILING PROBLEM

Both the teaching profession and the NHS operate structured pay scales. Teachers progress annually through six points on the Main Pay Scale (M1 to M6). NHS staff progress through defined points within their pay band. Scale progression adds meaningful salary increases in the early and middle career years — above and beyond the national pay award.

But both scales have a ceiling. Once a teacher reaches M6, further progression requires a formal application to the Upper Pay Scale, assessed against evidence of sustained high performance. It is not automatic, and many experienced teachers remain at M6 for years. Once an NHS Band 5 nurse reaches the top of their band, further progression requires applying for and being appointed to a Band 6 role. The national pay award is all they receive year on year.

At the ceiling, these workers are in exactly the same position as anyone else on a fixed grade: entirely dependent on national pay awards to keep pace with the cost of living.

THE TEACHER AT M6

A secondary school teacher in England, outside London. They reached M6 — the top of the Main Pay Scale — by the 2019/20 academic year. From that point, their only salary increases came from national pay awards. Scale progression was exhausted.

M6 SALARY, REST OF ENGLAND (CONFIRMED STPCD FIGURES): 2019/20: £40,035 2020/21: £41,136 (+2.75%) 2021/22: £41,136 (+0.0% — pay freeze applied) 2022/23: £43,685 (+5.0%) 2023/24: £46,525 (+6.5%) 2024/25: £49,084 (+5.5%) Total nominal increase 2019–2024: +£9,049 (+22.6%)

Now adjust for cumulative inflation of 26%. To maintain the same purchasing power as £40,035 in 2019/20, a salary of approximately £50,444 would be needed in 2024/25.

Actual 2024/25 salary: £49,084 Required to hold real terms: £50,444 Real-terms shortfall: −£1,360 (−2.7%)

The teacher’s nominal pay rose by over £9,000. In real terms — in what that salary actually buys — they are worse off than five years ago. The pay freeze of 2021/22, the single year with zero uplift, fell in the same period that inflation was beginning its rapid climb. That one year of no movement, when prices were already rising at 5.4%, created a gap that the subsequent years of awards have not fully closed.

Had the 2021/22 award matched inflation (5.4% rather than 0%), the additional salary that year would have been approximately £2,221. Compounded through subsequent years, the freeze year alone accounts for approximately £2,800 of cumulative shortfall.

THE BAND 5 NURSE AT THE TOP OF THEIR BAND

A registered nurse in England, outside London, who reached the top of NHS Band 5 by 2019/20. Like the teacher, further progression requires a different role (Band 6). National pay awards are their only annual increase.

To maintain real purchasing power from 2019/20, a 2024/25 salary of approximately £38,575 would be required.

Actual 2024/25 salary: £37,796 Required to hold real terms: £38,575 Real-terms shortfall: −£779 (−2.0%)

The nurse’s nominal pay rose by £7,181. In real terms they are approximately 2% worse off than in 2019/20. The near-freeze of 2020/21 — just 1% at the height of the pandemic — combined with the 3% award in 2021/22 when inflation was running at 5.4% created the gap.

The real-terms story for both workers is the same: nominal pay rose around 22–23%. Prices rose 26%. Both are behind.

NOW ADD FISCAL DRAG

The income tax position makes the picture materially worse. The key thresholds — the Personal Allowance and the Basic Rate limit — have been frozen since April 2021 and are scheduled to remain frozen until April 2028.

UK INCOME TAX THRESHOLDS (2024/25): Personal Allowance: £0–£12,570 taxed at 0% Basic Rate: £12,571–£50,270 taxed at 20% Higher Rate: £50,271+ taxed at 40% These thresholds have not moved since 2021. In a normal year they would rise with inflation. They have not.
Had the Personal Allowance been uprated with CPI from April 2021: Personal Allowance in 2024/25 (if uprated): approximately £14,700 Actual Personal Allowance: £12,570 Difference: £2,130 now taxed at 20% that would previously have been tax-free Extra income tax from frozen Personal Allowance alone: £2,130 × 20% = £426 per year
That £426 is not the result of a Budget announcement. It accrues silently because a threshold did not move.

WHY THE FREEZE HITS HARDER ON LOWER SALARIES

It might seem that fiscal drag primarily affects higher earners being pulled toward the 40% threshold. In fact the Personal Allowance freeze hits lower and middle earners proportionally harder.

The Personal Allowance represents a larger share of a £30,000 salary than a £80,000 salary. When £2,130 that should be tax-free is instead taxed at 20%, the £426 cost represents 1.4% of the nurse’s gross salary and just 0.5% of a senior manager’s. The freeze is regressive — it extracts a larger proportion from those who can least afford it.

THE SAME ARITHMETIC APPLIES TO MILLIONS

The teacher at M6 and the nurse at the top of Band 5 are not unusual cases. They represent the most common situation for experienced public sector workers: scale progression exhausted, reliant entirely on national awards, watching inflation erode what those awards gained.

The same calculation applies to a police constable at the top of their scale. A council worker at the ceiling of their grade. A civil servant who has not been promoted. Any worker on a structured scale who has hit the top — and there are hundreds of thousands of them — faces exactly this arithmetic.

WHAT YOU CAN DO ABOUT IT

Fiscal drag and inflation are structural policy decisions, not personal finance failures. But within the constraints you face, two tools reduce the damage.

Salary sacrifice into your pension reduces your taxable income before tax and National Insurance are calculated. For the nurse earning £37,796, sacrificing £3,000 into their pension reduces their taxable income by £3,000 — saving approximately £600 in income tax and £240 in NI, a combined saving of £840 per year. Their take-home falls by just £2,160 to generate £3,000 of pension saving. The government and HMRC effectively contribute £840 of that pension for free. FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

What is fiscal drag and how does it work?

Fiscal drag occurs when income tax thresholds are frozen while wages rise. As salaries increase — even modestly — more income falls into higher tax brackets automatically. The UK Personal Allowance and Basic Rate limit have been frozen since 2021 and are scheduled to remain frozen until 2028, silently increasing the tax burden on every worker whose pay rises.

Why does freezing tax thresholds increase the amount I pay in tax?

Because the Personal Allowance (£12,570) has not risen with inflation. If it had been uprated with CPI, it would be approximately £14,700 in 2024/25. The £2,130 difference is now taxed at 20%, costing every worker above the threshold an extra £426 per year — with no announcement, no vote, and no visibility on the payslip.

How do I calculate my real wage growth after inflation?

Subtract the cumulative inflation rate from your cumulative pay rise. If your salary has risen 22% since 2019 but prices have risen 26%, your real wage growth is approximately −4%. The Take Home Pay Calculator can show the exact impact on your net pay.

What is the best way to reduce the impact of fiscal drag on my take-home pay?

Salary sacrifice into your pension is the most effective tool. It reduces your taxable income before tax and National Insurance are calculated, effectively recovering some of the fiscal drag. For a worker earning £37,796, sacrificing £3,000 saves approximately £840 in combined tax and NI.
