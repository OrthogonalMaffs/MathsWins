HOW THE MATHS WORKS

Monthly Interest

Every month, each debt accrues interest based on its APR. The formula is straightforward:

Monthly interest = Balance × (APR / 100 / 12)

A £5,000 credit card at 22.9% APR generates £95.42 in interest every single month. That is money which reduces nothing — it just services the cost of borrowing.

The Snowball vs Avalanche Difference

Both methods pay the same minimum payments on every debt. The only difference is where the extra payment goes:

Snowball: Extra goes to the smallest balance. You eliminate debts quickly, freeing up their minimum payments to roll into the next debt. Each payoff accelerates the next — like a snowball rolling downhill.

Avalanche: Extra goes to the highest APR. This minimises total interest paid because you are attacking the most expensive debt first. Mathematically optimal, but the first payoff can take longer.

Why Compounding Makes High-APR Debt So Expensive

Interest compounds monthly. Each month's interest is added to the balance, and next month you pay interest on that interest. At 22.9% APR, a £5,000 balance left untouched grows to £6,274 in one year — you owe £1,274 more without borrowing a penny.

This is why the avalanche method saves money: it stops the most aggressive compounding first.

WORKED EXAMPLE: THREE DEBTS, £200/MONTH EXTRA

DebtBalanceAPRMin Payment
Store Card£80029.9%£25
Credit Card£4,20022.9%£85
Car Loan£8,5006.9%£180

Snowball order: Store Card (£800) → Credit Card (£4,200) → Car Loan (£8,500)

The store card is gone in about 4 months. That frees up £225/month for the credit card, which clears in roughly 15 more months. Then £490/month hits the car loan.

Avalanche order: Store Card (29.9%) → Credit Card (22.9%) → Car Loan (6.9%)

In this case, snowball and avalanche happen to agree — the smallest balance also has the highest APR. When they disagree, avalanche saves more money but snowball delivers the first payoff sooner.

The mathematically optimal strategy is always avalanche. But research consistently shows that people who use the snowball method are more likely to become debt-free. The best strategy is the one you will actually follow through on.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

What is the debt snowball method?

Pay off your smallest debt first while making minimum payments on everything else. When the smallest is gone, roll that payment into the next smallest. Quick wins build psychological momentum.

What is the debt avalanche method?

Pay off your highest APR debt first. Mathematically, this saves the most money in total interest. It can feel slower because the biggest rate isn't always the smallest balance.

Which is better — snowball or avalanche?

Avalanche saves more money. Snowball gives faster early wins which keeps people motivated. Research shows people who use snowball are more likely to become debt-free because they don't give up. The best method is the one you'll stick with.

How much extra should I pay toward debt?

Even £50/month extra makes a significant difference. This calculator shows you exactly how much time and money any extra payment saves.

Does the snowball method really work?

Yes. A 2016 Harvard Business Review study found that people who focused on paying off small balances first were more likely to eliminate their total debt than those who tried to optimise mathematically. Motivation matters more than optimisation.

Should I save or pay off debt first?

If your debt APR is higher than your savings interest rate, paying off debt first is mathematically better. But always keep a small emergency fund (£1,000) before aggressively paying down debt.

What about loans where interest is added upfront?

Some lenders (like Oakbrook) calculate all interest at the start and add it to your balance. Your monthly payments just divide the total by the number of months — no ongoing interest calculation. For these loans, tick "Interest already added to balance" and the calculator treats it correctly. The balance you owe is already the full amount including interest.
