HOW THE MATHS WORKS

Child Benefit Rates (2025/26)

Child Benefit is paid weekly. The first child receives a higher rate, and each additional child receives a lower rate. These rates are set by HMRC each tax year.

First child: £26.05/week = £1,354.60/year
Each additional child: £17.25/week = £897.00/year

For a family with three children, the total would be £1,354.60 + (2 × £897.00) = £3,148.60 per year. This is tax-free income — unless the High Income Child Benefit Charge applies.

The High Income Child Benefit Charge

If either parent (not the household — the individual) earns over £60,000, HMRC claws back Child Benefit through the tax system. The clawback rate is precise:

Clawback = 1% of total Child Benefit for every £200 income exceeds £60,000

This means the clawback is linear: at £62,000 you lose 10%. At £70,000 you lose 50%. At £80,000 or above, 100% is clawed back. The charge is collected via Self Assessment.

The Pension Sacrifice Strategy

Salary sacrifice into a workplace pension reduces your adjusted net income for HICBC purposes. If you earn £65,000 and sacrifice £500/month (£6,000/year), your adjusted income drops to £59,000 — below the threshold. You keep the full Child Benefit, gain tax relief on the pension contribution, and avoid the Self Assessment requirement for HICBC.

This is not tax avoidance — it is exactly how the system is designed to work. HMRC explicitly uses adjusted net income (after pension contributions) for the HICBC calculation.

The NI Credits Trap

This is the part most people miss. A parent who is not working (or earning below the NI threshold) and who claims Child Benefit automatically receives Class 3 National Insurance credits. These credits count toward their State Pension qualification. Each missing qualifying year reduces the State Pension by approximately £329 per year — for life, from age 67 onwards.

Even if the HICBC claws back 100% of the benefit, the NI credits are still awarded. You can claim Child Benefit and immediately opt out of receiving payments. This gives the non-working parent NI credits without triggering the HICBC or requiring a Self Assessment return.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

What is the HICBC threshold and how does the clawback work?

The High Income Child Benefit Charge starts when either parent earns over £60,000 (from April 2024 — it was £50,000 before). For every £200 of income above £60,000, you lose 1% of your Child Benefit. At £80,000 the entire benefit is clawed back. This is based on individual income, not combined household income.

Can pension sacrifice help me avoid the HICBC?

Yes. Salary sacrifice into a workplace pension reduces your adjusted net income. If this brings the higher earner below £60,000, the full Child Benefit is kept with zero clawback. You also get tax relief on the pension contribution. For many families this is a significant net gain — more pension, more Child Benefit, less tax.

Why should I always claim Child Benefit, even if it is fully clawed back?

Because claiming Child Benefit provides National Insurance credits to the non-working or lower-earning parent. These credits count toward State Pension entitlement. Each missing qualifying year costs approximately £329 per year of pension income for life. You can claim and opt out of payments to get the NI credits without triggering the charge.

Do single parents have to worry about the HICBC?

Yes. The HICBC is based on the individual income of the claimant (or their partner, if they have one). For single parents, it is based solely on their own income. If a single parent earns under £60,000, they keep the full benefit with no clawback.

How do I actually claim Child Benefit?

You can claim online via the gov.uk website or by post using form CH2. Claim as soon as possible after your child is born or comes to live with you — it can be backdated up to 3 months. If you want the NI credits but not the payments, claim and then opt out of receiving payments online.

Do I need to file a Self Assessment tax return for the HICBC?

If you or your partner earns over £60,000 and you receive Child Benefit payments, the higher earner must register for Self Assessment and file a return to pay the HICBC. However, if you claim Child Benefit but opt out of receiving payments (to get NI credits only), no Self Assessment is required for HICBC purposes.
