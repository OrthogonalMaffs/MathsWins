HOW SIDE HUSTLE TAX ACTUALLY WORKS

Your Side Income Sits on Top

For tax purposes, your side hustle profit is added on top of your employment income. This means it is taxed at whatever your highest marginal rate happens to be — not from zero. If your salary already uses up your Personal Allowance and basic rate band, every pound of side income is taxed at 40% or even 45%.

The Marginal Rate Trap

Many people assume side income is taxed separately at 20%. It is not. Your total income determines your tax band, and side income fills up from wherever your salary left off. A basic rate taxpayer earning £45,000 has £5,270 of basic rate band remaining. Any side income beyond that crosses into the 40% higher rate band.

Marginal tax on side income = Total tax WITH side income − Total tax WITHOUT side income

The £100k Personal Allowance Trap

If your combined income crosses £100,000, you lose £1 of Personal Allowance for every £2 over £100k. This creates an effective 60% tax rate on income between £100,000 and £125,140. A side hustle pushing you into this zone can be devastatingly expensive in tax.

Effective rate between £100k–£125,140 = 40% income tax + 20% PA clawback = 60%

National Insurance — The One People Forget

Self-employment profits above £12,570 attract both Class 2 NI (£3.45/week flat) and Class 4 NI (6% on profits between £12,570 and £50,270, then 2% above that). This is on top of income tax and is separate from the NI your employer deducts from your salary.

Payment on Account

After your first Self Assessment, HMRC demands advance payments. In January and July, you pay 50% of your previous year's tax bill as an advance for the current year. This catches many first-time side hustlers off guard — your first January bill can be 150% of what you expected.

The key insight: your side income is not taxed in isolation. It sits on top of your salary, which determines whether you pay 20%, 40%, 45%, or even 60%. Always calculate the marginal impact — total tax with side income minus total tax without it.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

Do I need to pay tax on my side hustle?

If your side income exceeds £1,000 (the Trading Allowance), yes. You will need to register for Self Assessment and file a tax return. Below £1,000, you don't need to report it at all.

How much tax will I pay on side income?

It depends on your employment income. Your side income is taxed at your marginal rate — 20% if you are a basic rate taxpayer, 40% if higher rate. Plus Class 2 and Class 4 National Insurance on self-employment profits above £12,570.

What is the £1,000 Trading Allowance?

HMRC lets you earn up to £1,000 from self-employment without paying tax or reporting it. If you earn more, you can either deduct the £1,000 allowance OR deduct your actual expenses — whichever saves you more.

Do I pay National Insurance on side income?

Yes, if profits exceed £12,570. Class 2 NI is a flat £3.45/week (£179.40/year). Class 4 NI is 6% on profits between £12,570 and £50,270, then 2% above that. This is separate from the NI your employer deducts from your salary.

When do I need to register for Self Assessment?

By 5 October after the end of the tax year in which you started your side hustle. File your return by 31 January (online). Late registration and late filing both carry penalties.

What expenses can I deduct?

Anything “wholly and exclusively” for your side hustle: equipment, materials, software, phone (business proportion), travel to clients, professional training, home office costs (simplified: £6/week or £26/month without receipts).
