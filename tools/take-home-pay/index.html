UNDERSTANDING YOUR PAYSLIP

The Journey From Gross to Net

Your gross salary is what your employer pays for your role. But before money reaches your bank account, several deductions are taken. Income tax is collected under PAYE (Pay As You Earn), National Insurance funds the state pension and NHS, and any student loan repayment is collected automatically once you earn above the threshold.

Why Marginal Rates Matter More Than Average Rates

Your average tax rate is total tax divided by total income. Your marginal rate is the tax on your next pound of income. This distinction matters because it tells you how much of a pay rise you actually keep. A 40% taxpayer getting a £1,000 raise keeps £520 after tax and NI — not the £780 that the average rate might suggest.

Marginal rate = Income tax rate + NI rate + Student loan rate on your next £1

The Pension Contribution Trick

Every £1 you put into a pension costs you less than £1 from your pocket. With salary sacrifice, a basic-rate taxpayer putting in £100 only loses £72 from their take-home pay (they save £20 income tax + £8 NI). A higher-rate taxpayer loses only £58 for the same £100 pension contribution.

This is mathematics, not a loophole. The government deliberately incentivises pension saving through tax relief.

Why the £100k Trap Exists

The personal allowance (£12,570 of tax-free income) is withdrawn at a rate of £1 for every £2 earned above £100,000. Between £100,000 and £125,140, you effectively pay 60% tax on each additional pound (40% income tax + 20% from lost allowance). Pension contributions can bring your adjusted income below £100,000, restoring your full personal allowance. The maths can be striking: a £1,000 pension contribution in this band could save £600 in tax.

Understanding your marginal rate is the single most useful piece of financial mathematics you can learn. It determines the real cost of pension contributions, the real value of a pay rise, and whether you are near a threshold worth optimising around.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

How is take-home pay calculated?

Your employer deducts income tax, National Insurance, pension contributions, and student loan repayments from your gross salary. What's left is your take-home (net) pay. This calculator uses 2025/26 HMRC rates and thresholds.

What tax code should I use?

Most employees are on 1257L, which gives you the standard £12,570 personal allowance. Check your payslip or P60. If your code ends in L, multiply the number by 10 to get your personal allowance. Other codes (BR, D0, K codes) work differently — this calculator interprets L, T, and 0T codes. Use 1257L if unsure.

How does salary sacrifice save tax?

With salary sacrifice, your pension contribution is deducted before tax AND National Insurance are calculated. This means you save NI (8%) on top of income tax relief. Relief at source only gets income tax relief — you still pay NI on the full amount.

Do I pay tax on my pension contributions?

No — pension contributions are tax-free. With salary sacrifice, you never pay tax on that money. With relief at source, you pay it but get 20% back automatically (your provider claims it from HMRC). Higher-rate taxpayers can claim the extra relief via self-assessment.

How does the £100,000 personal allowance trap work?

For every £2 you earn above £100,000, you lose £1 of personal allowance. Between £100,000 and £125,140, your effective marginal rate is 60% (40% tax + 20% from the lost allowance). Pension contributions can bring your adjusted income below £100,000 to avoid this trap entirely.

Are Scottish tax rates different?

Yes. Scotland has six income tax bands instead of three. The starter rate is 19% (lower than England's 20%) but the higher rate is 42% (vs 40% in England) and the top rate is 48% (vs 45%). The crossover point depends on your income. This calculator handles both correctly — tick the Scotland checkbox to use Scottish rates.
