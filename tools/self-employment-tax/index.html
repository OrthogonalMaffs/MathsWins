HOW SELF-EMPLOYMENT TAX WORKS

The Three Taxes You Pay

As a sole trader, your profit (turnover minus expenses) is subject to three separate taxes: income tax, Class 2 National Insurance, and Class 4 National Insurance. Each has different thresholds and rates.

Income Tax

Your self-employment profit is added to any other income (employment salary, etc.) to determine your total taxable income. Everyone gets a £12,570 personal allowance — you pay no tax on the first £12,570.

Basic rate: 20% on £12,571 – £50,270
Higher rate: 40% on £50,271 – £125,140
Additional rate: 45% above £125,140

If your total income exceeds £100,000, your personal allowance is reduced by £1 for every £2 above £100,000. This creates an effective 60% marginal rate between £100,000 and £125,140.

National Insurance

Class 2 NI is a flat £3.45 per week (£179.40/year) if your profits exceed £12,570. Class 4 NI is 6% on profits between £12,570 and £50,270, then 2% above that.

Class 2: £3.45/week (if profit > £12,570)
Class 4: 6% on £12,570 – £50,270, 2% above £50,270

Payment on Account

After your first tax return, HMRC assumes you will earn a similar amount next year and asks for advance payments. In January, you pay your outstanding balance PLUS 50% of next year’s estimated tax. In July, you pay the other 50%. This means your second year of self-employment can feel like paying 150% of your tax bill.

The single biggest cash-flow surprise for new sole traders is Payment on Account. If your first year’s tax bill is £6,000, your January payment will be £6,000 + £3,000 = £9,000. Budget for it from day one.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

How much tax do I pay as self-employed?

Income tax on profits above £12,570, plus Class 2 NI (£3.45/week) and Class 4 NI (6% then 2%). Total is typically 29–42% depending on profit level.

What is Payment on Account?

HMRC’s advance payment system. After your first year, you pay 50% of next year’s estimated tax in January and 50% in July, on top of this year’s balance. Budget for 150% of your tax bill in year two.

What expenses can I claim?

Anything “wholly and exclusively” for business: materials, travel, phone (business %), equipment, insurance, accountant fees. Keep receipts for everything.

Do I need an accountant?

Not legally required, but most self-employed people save more in tax deductions than the accountant costs. An accountant typically costs £200–£500/year for a sole trader.

When do I need to register?

Register for Self Assessment by 5 October after the tax year you started. File by 31 January (online). Late registration carries penalties.

Can I reduce my tax bill legally?

Yes. Maximise allowable expenses, use pension contributions (tax-deductible), consider timing income across tax years, and claim all applicable reliefs.
