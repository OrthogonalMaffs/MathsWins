HOW THE MATHS WORKS

The Income Multiplier Approach

The simplest rule of thumb is "10 times your salary." It is a decent starting point, but it ignores your actual circumstances. A single person with no mortgage needs far less than a parent of three with a large outstanding loan.

The Needs-Based Calculation

This calculator uses the needs-based method — the approach financial advisers actually use. It adds up everything your family would need if you died tomorrow, then subtracts what they already have.

Cover needed = (Income × Years) + Mortgage + Debts + Funeral + Emergency + (Uni × Children) − Existing cover − (Partner income × Years)

The result is the gap between what your family needs and what they currently have. That gap is your cover amount.

Why We Round Up

Insurance policies are sold in round numbers. If you need £372,750, you would buy £375,000. Rounding up to the nearest £25,000 gives a small buffer for inflation and unexpected costs — and the premium difference is negligible.

Term Length

Match your term to the longest obligation. If your youngest child is 3 and you want them covered until 21, that is 18 years. If your mortgage runs for 22 more years, go with 25. The term should outlast your biggest financial commitment.

Life insurance is one of the few financial products where the maths is genuinely simple. The hard part is not the calculation — it is sitting down and doing it. You have now done it.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

How much life insurance do I actually need?

It depends on your debts, income, dependents, and existing cover. The "10 times salary" rule is a rough guide, but this calculator gives you a personalised figure based on your real numbers. Most families in the UK need between £200,000 and £500,000 of cover.

What is the difference between term and whole-of-life insurance?

Term insurance covers a fixed period (e.g. 20 years) and only pays out if you die during the term. Whole-of-life always pays out but costs 5–10 times more. For most people, term insurance matched to their mortgage or child-rearing years is far better value. You are insuring against dying too soon, not too late.

Do I need life insurance if I am single with no dependents?

Probably not. Without dependents, nobody suffers financially if you die. You might want a small policy for funeral costs and any debts. However, if you plan to have a family, locking in a low premium while young and healthy can save you money long-term.

Is employer death-in-service benefit enough?

It is a good start — typically 1–4 times your salary — but it ends the moment you leave the company. Redundancy, career change, or starting a business means losing that cover, often at an age when new policies are more expensive. A personal policy stays with you regardless.

When should I review my life insurance?

Whenever your circumstances change: new mortgage, new baby, pay rise, divorce, debt paid off, partner stopping work. At a minimum, review every 3–5 years. A policy that was right five years ago could easily be £100,000 too low or too high today.
