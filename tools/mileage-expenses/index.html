HOW SELF-EMPLOYED EXPENSES WORK

The Basic Principle

As a sole trader, you pay tax on your profit — not your total income. Every legitimate business expense reduces your taxable profit, which reduces both income tax and Class 4 National Insurance.

Tax saving = Claimable expenses × (tax rate + NI rate)

If you are a higher-rate taxpayer claiming £5,000 in expenses, that is £5,000 × 46% = £2,300 less tax. The expenses do not come off your tax bill directly — they come off the profit that gets taxed.

HMRC Simplified Mileage Rates

HMRC lets you claim a flat rate per business mile instead of tracking actual vehicle costs. This is usually simpler and often more generous for moderate mileage.

First 10,000 miles: 45p/mile (cars/vans)
After 10,000 miles: 25p/mile (cars/vans)
Motorcycles: 24p/mile (all miles)
Bicycles: 20p/mile (all miles)

The 45p rate covers fuel, insurance, road tax, MOT, servicing, and depreciation. If you use the simplified rate, you cannot also claim these costs separately. Choose one method or the other.

Home Office: Simplified vs Actual

The simplified method gives you £6 per week (£312/year) with zero paperwork. The actual method requires you to calculate the business proportion of your home costs — more effort, but potentially a larger claim if your home costs are high or you use a significant proportion of your home for business.

Most self-employed people under-claim. The biggest missed deductions are mileage (people forget to log journeys), home office (people do not realise they can claim), and professional development (courses, books, and memberships).

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

How much can I claim per mile?

45p per mile for the first 10,000 business miles in a car or van, then 25p per mile after that. This covers fuel, insurance, wear and tear — you cannot claim these separately if using the mileage rate.

Can I claim for working from home?

Yes. The simplest method is £6 per week (£312/year) with no receipts needed. Or calculate the actual proportion of home costs used for business — more work but potentially more money.

What counts as a business mile?

Travel to client sites, supplier visits, business meetings. NOT your regular commute to a fixed workplace. If you work from home, travel to clients counts from your home.

Do I need receipts for everything?

For mileage, keep a log of business journeys (date, destination, miles, purpose). For other expenses, keep receipts for 5 years. The simplified home office rate needs no receipts.

Can I claim for my phone?

Yes — the business-use proportion. If you use your phone 50% for business, claim 50% of the bill. HMRC accepts reasonable estimates but keep your bills.

What expenses are NOT claimable?

Client entertaining (meals/drinks), clothing (unless uniform/protective), fines and penalties, your own labour, personal items. If in doubt, the HMRC test is: “wholly and exclusively for business purposes.”
