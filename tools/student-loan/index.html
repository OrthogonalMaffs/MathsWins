HOW STUDENT LOAN REPAYMENT ACTUALLY WORKS

Your student loan is not like a mortgage or a credit card. It behaves like a graduate tax with an expiry date. Understanding this changes how you think about it.

You only repay above the threshold. On Plan 2, you repay 9% of earnings above £28,470 — not 9% of your whole salary. Earning £30,000 means repaying 9% of £1,530 = £137.70/year = £11.48/month. That's it.
It gets written off. Plan 2 loans are written off 30 years after you become eligible to repay (usually the April after you graduate). If you graduated in 2022, your loan is written off in April 2053 regardless of the remaining balance. You owe nothing after that date.
Interest accrues from day one. While you're studying, Plan 2 charges RPI + 3%. After graduating, the rate scales from RPI (at the threshold) to RPI + 3% (at £52,885+). At current RPI of 3.2%, that's 3.2% to 6.2% depending on your income. For many borrowers, the balance grows faster than they repay it — and that's by design. The write-off is the intended exit for most.

THE KEY QUESTION: SHOULD YOU OVERPAY?

This depends entirely on whether you'll clear the balance before the write-off date. Use the calculator above to find out.

If your loan will be written off:
Every pound you overpay is a pound you would have never had to repay anyway.
Overpaying is literally giving money away.
→ Do NOT overpay.

If you'll clear the balance before write-off:
Overpaying reduces the total interest you pay.
But compare the interest rate to your alternatives.
→ Overpay only if the loan interest exceeds your savings/investment returns.
For the estimated 83% of Plan 2 borrowers who will never repay in full, overpaying is mathematically equivalent to burning money. The write-off date is your friend. Treat the repayment as a tax — 9% on earnings above the threshold — and forget about the balance. The balance is a number on a screen that will eventually be set to zero regardless of how big it is.
The exception: If you're close to clearing the balance and the remaining amount is modest relative to your income, paying it off early saves interest. The calculator shows you whether you're in this position.

COMBINED MARGINAL RATES

A student loan repayment stacks on top of income tax and NI. Your actual marginal rate on each extra pound earned:

Basic rate taxpayer + Plan 2:
20% IT + 8% NI + 9% student loan = 37%

Higher rate taxpayer + Plan 2:
40% IT + 2% NI + 9% student loan = 51%

Higher rate + Plan 2 + Postgraduate:
40% IT + 2% NI + 9% UG + 6% PG = 57%

£100k–£125k band + Plan 2:
62% effective IT/NI + 9% student loan = 71%
A higher-rate taxpayer with both an undergraduate and postgraduate loan keeps just 43p of every extra pound they earn. In the £100k–£125k band with a student loan, you keep 29p. These are real marginal rates that affect real take-home pay — and they link directly to the tax analysis in our UK Tax Maths course.
