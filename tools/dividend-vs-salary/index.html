HOW THE MATHS WORKS

The Core Trade-off

As a limited company director, you have two ways to extract money: salary and dividends. Each is taxed differently, and the optimal strategy exploits the gap between them.

Salary: Deductible but Expensive

Salary is a company expense — it reduces your corporation tax bill. But it attracts income tax (20–45%) and National Insurance (both employee and employer). The combined marginal rate on salary above the personal allowance can reach 33.25% or more before you even count corp tax savings.

Salary cost = Income tax + Employee NI + Employer NI − Corp tax saved

Dividends: Cheaper but Not Deductible

Dividends are paid from post-tax profits, so the company pays corporation tax first. But the personal tax rate on dividends is much lower: 8.75% basic vs 20% income tax, and no National Insurance at all.

Dividend cost = Corporation tax (19–25%) + Dividend tax (8.75–39.35%)

The Sweet Spot

Take exactly £12,570 as salary (the personal allowance). You pay zero income tax on it, zero employee NI (below the primary threshold), and the company saves 19–25% in corp tax because salary is deductible. Everything above that comes out as dividends at 8.75% basic rate.

This is why hundreds of thousands of UK directors pay themselves this way. It is not a loophole — it is how the tax system is designed.

The difference between all-salary and the optimal split typically saves a director between £5,000 and £15,000 per year. On £80,000 profit, the saving is usually around £7,000 — money that stays in your pocket instead of going to HMRC.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

What's the most tax-efficient salary for a director?

Usually £12,570 per year (the personal allowance). This uses your tax-free allowance, avoids employee NI, and reduces corporation tax.

Why take dividends instead of salary?

Dividend tax rates (8.75% basic) are much lower than income tax + NI (20% + 8% = 28%). The trade-off: dividends don't count toward pension or mortgage applications as reliably.

Do I still pay tax on dividends?

Yes, but at lower rates. The first £1,000 is tax-free (dividend allowance). Then 8.75% basic rate, 33.75% higher rate, 39.35% additional rate. Much less than salary.

What about corporation tax?

Salary is a deductible expense, so it reduces your corp tax bill. Dividends are paid from post-tax profits. The optimal split balances both.

How does this affect my mortgage application?

Some lenders accept salary + dividends, others prefer salary only. If you're planning a mortgage, discuss with your broker before changing your pay structure.

Is this legal?

Completely. Paying yourself a mix of salary and dividends is standard practice for hundreds of thousands of UK limited company directors. It's tax-efficient, not tax avoidance.
