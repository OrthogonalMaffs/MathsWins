HOW PENSION MATHS WORKS

The Power of Compound Growth

Compound growth is the single most important force in pension mathematics. Your contributions earn returns, then those returns earn returns, then those returns earn returns. Given enough time, the growth dwarfs the contributions themselves.

Future Value = P × (1 + r)n + C × [((1 + r)n - 1) / r]

Where P is your starting pot, r is the monthly growth rate, n is the number of months, and C is your monthly contribution.

Here is why starting early matters so much:

Why Employer Match Is the Best Return in Finance

If your employer matches your pension contributions, that match is an instant 100% return on your money. No investment in history consistently delivers 100% returns — but employer matching does, every single month.

If you earn £35,000 and your employer matches up to 5%, that is £1,750 per year in free money. Over 32 years at 5% growth, that employer match alone grows to roughly £131,000. Not contributing enough to get the full employer match is leaving salary on the table.

The State Pension as Your Foundation

The full new state pension is £11,502 per year (2025/26). It provides a baseline — but it replaces only about 33% of the average UK salary. Think of it as the foundation, not the whole building. Your private pension needs to fill the gap between the state pension and the income you actually want in retirement.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

How much pension do I need to retire?

A common rule of thumb is 67% of your pre-retirement salary. So if you earn £35,000, aim for ~£23,000/year in retirement. This calculator shows you whether you're on track.

What is the 4% rule?

Based on the Trinity Study, if you withdraw 4% of your pension pot in year one and adjust for inflation each year, your money has historically lasted 30+ years. £400,000 pot = £16,000/year withdrawal.

How much should I contribute to my pension?

Auto-enrolment minimum is 8% total (5% you + 3% employer). Financial advisers often recommend 12-15% total to retire comfortably. The earlier you start, the less you need to save due to compound growth.

Does my employer contribution count?

Yes — and it is essentially free money. If your employer matches up to 5%, not contributing at least 5% is leaving salary on the table. This calculator includes both your and your employer's contributions.

What is the state pension in 2025/26?

The full new state pension is £11,502 per year (£221.20 per week). You need 35 qualifying years of National Insurance to get the full amount. This calculator adds the state pension to your private pension income.

When can I access my pension?

Currently 55 (rising to 57 in 2028). But accessing early gives your pot less time to grow. This calculator shows the impact of different retirement ages.
