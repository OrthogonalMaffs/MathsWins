THE MATHS OF SAVING

Simple vs Compound Growth

If you save £200 per month with no interest, after 5 years you have £12,000. Simple enough. But with compound interest, your savings earn interest, and then that interest earns interest. This is the most powerful force in personal finance.

Future Value = PMT × [((1 + r)^n - 1) / r] + PV × (1 + r)^n

Where PMT is your monthly payment, r is the monthly interest rate (annual rate / 12), n is the number of months, and PV is your starting balance. The first term is the future value of your regular contributions. The second is the growth of what you already have.

Why Consistency Beats Amount

Saving £100 every single month for 10 years at 4% gives you £14,725. Saving £200 a month for 5 years at the same rate gives you £13,260. The person who saved half as much per month but stuck with it twice as long came out ahead. Time in the market beats timing the market. Time saving beats the amount saved.

The Rule of 72

Want to know how long it takes your money to double? Divide 72 by the interest rate. At 4%, your money doubles in roughly 18 years. At 6%, roughly 12 years. At 8%, roughly 9 years. This is a quick mental shortcut for understanding the power of compound growth.

Doubling time (years) ≈ 72 / interest rate (%)

The Cost of Waiting

Starting one year earlier on £200/month at 5% means an extra £3,800 after 10 years — not just £2,400 in contributions, but £1,400 in compound growth you would have missed. Every month you delay is a month your money cannot work for you. The best time to start was yesterday. The second best time is today.

Compound interest is mathematics working in your favour. Every month you save, the gap between effort and reward gets wider — in the right direction. Start small if you must, but start.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

How much should I save each month?

A common guideline is to save at least 20% of your take-home pay (the 50/30/20 rule). But the real answer depends on your goal. This calculator works backwards from your target amount and deadline to give you a precise monthly figure. Even saving a small amount consistently is far better than saving nothing.

Does compound interest really make a big difference on savings?

Yes, especially over longer timeframes. At 4% annual interest, saving £200 per month for 10 years gives you £29,490 — but only £24,000 of that is your contributions. The remaining £5,490 is free money from compound interest. Over 20 years, interest contributes over £25,000 on the same monthly saving.

What interest rate should I use in the calculator?

Use the AER (Annual Equivalent Rate) of your savings account. In 2025-26, easy-access accounts typically offer 3-5%, while fixed-rate ISAs can offer 4-5%. If you are investing rather than saving, historical stock market returns average around 7-10% before inflation, but with much higher risk.

Should I save or pay off debt first?

If your debt charges more interest than your savings earn (which is almost always the case), paying off debt first is mathematically better. However, most financial advisers recommend keeping a small emergency fund of £1,000 before aggressively paying down debt. Use our Debt Snowball Calculator to optimise your debt payoff strategy.

How accurate is this savings calculator?

The calculator uses standard compound interest formulae with monthly compounding. Real-world results may differ slightly due to variable interest rates, tax on savings interest above your Personal Savings Allowance, and the timing of deposits within each month. It gives you an excellent planning estimate.
