HOW THE MATHS WORKS

The True Cost of Renting

Rent is not just £X per month. Rent escalates. At 3% annual increases, £1,200/month becomes £1,619/month after 10 years and £2,184/month after 20 years. The total rent paid over 25 years at these rates is over £437,000 — none of which builds equity.

But a renter who invests the deposit they would have used to buy is not standing still. A £30,000 deposit invested at 7% annual return compounds to £162,637 after 25 years. That is the opportunity cost a buyer gives up.

The True Cost of Buying

Buying involves costs that go far beyond the mortgage payment:

Total cost = Mortgage payments + Stamp duty + Maintenance − Property value growth

On a £270,000 mortgage at 5% over 25 years, you pay £473,486 in total — that is £203,486 in interest alone. Add 1% annual maintenance on a property growing at 3% per year, plus stamp duty of £2,500 on a £300,000 property, and the true outlay is enormous.

However, the buyer builds equity. After 25 years the mortgage is gone, and if the house grew at 3% per year the property is worth £627,892. That equity is the buyer’s return.

Mortgage Amortisation

Early mortgage payments are almost entirely interest. On a £270,000 mortgage at 5%, your first month’s payment of £1,578 includes £1,125 of interest and only £453 of principal. It takes years before the majority of each payment goes toward the actual debt.

Monthly payment = P × r × (1 + r)^n / ((1 + r)^n − 1)
where P = loan, r = monthly rate, n = total months

Stamp Duty

UK stamp duty (SDLT) applies in bands: 0% up to £125,000, then 2% on the next £125,000, 5% on £250,001–£925,000, and so on. On a £300,000 property, stamp duty is £2,500. This is a sunk cost — it never comes back.

Buying is not always better. If rent is low relative to the property price, investing the deposit can outperform property equity. This calculator shows your specific numbers — not generalisations, not rules of thumb, just the maths.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

When is renting better than buying?

Renting can be better when the rent-to-price ratio is low (monthly rent is small relative to the property price), when investment returns exceed house price growth, when you plan to move within a few years, or when the deposit could earn more invested in the stock market. In expensive cities, renting and investing often wins over 10–15 years.

When is buying better than renting?

Buying typically wins over longer time horizons (15–25 years) because mortgage payments build equity while rent payments do not. It also wins when house price growth is strong, when rent increases are high, and when you plan to stay in one place for a long time. Once the mortgage is paid off, your housing costs drop dramatically.

What about a house price crash?

House prices can fall — they dropped roughly 15–20% in 2008–09. If you buy at a peak and need to sell during a downturn, you could face negative equity. Try setting house price growth to 0% or negative to see how your numbers change. Over 25 years, even with a crash, UK property has historically recovered and grown.

Does the size of the deposit matter?

Absolutely. A larger deposit means a smaller mortgage (less interest paid) and often a better mortgage rate. But it also means a larger sum that could have been invested elsewhere. The opportunity cost of locking up £60,000 in a house versus investing it at 7% per year is significant. This calculator accounts for that trade-off.

What about the emotional value of owning a home?

This calculator deals with the mathematics only. Owning a home provides stability, the freedom to renovate, and a sense of security that has real value — but it cannot be quantified. Some people will happily accept a worse financial outcome for the certainty of owning their home. That is a perfectly rational choice — just make it with your eyes open on the numbers.
